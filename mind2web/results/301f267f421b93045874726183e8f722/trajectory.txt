# 24 Healthy Vegan Snack Ideas

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/12/Lizzie-Streit-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Written by Lizzie Streit, MS, RDN, LD on November 12, 2018

## 

Coming up with healthy snack ideas that fit a vegan diet can be challenging.

This is because the vegan diet includes only plant foods and excludes all animal products, limiting the selection of snack foods.

Luckily, countless combinations of plant foods can make up healthy and satisfying snacks — whether you eat fully vegan or are simply interested in reducing animal products in your diet.

Here are 24 healthy vegan snacks that are both tasty and nutritious.

![](//i0.wp.com/images-prod.healthline.com/hlcmsresource/images/AN_images/vegan-snacks-1296x728-feature.jpg?w=1155&h=1528)[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fvegan-snacks&media=https%3A%2F%2Fimages-prod.healthline.com%2Fhlcmsresource%2Fimages%2FAN_images%2Fvegan-snacks-1296x728-feature.jpg&description=24%20Healthy%20Vegan%20Snack%20Ideas "Share on Pinterest")

## 

1\. Fruit and Nut Butter

Fruit and nut butter, made from blended nuts, is a delicious vegan snack with many nutritional benefits.

Fruits provide fiber, vitamins and minerals, while nut butters are rich in fiber and protein that can help you feel full and energized ([1](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/1846/2), [2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0924224414002386), [3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10721886)).

Popular combinations include bananas or [apples](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-health-benefits-of-apples) with cashew, almond or [peanut butter](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-peanut-butter-bad-for-you).

For the most nutritional benefits, make sure to select a nut butter without added sugar, oil or salt.

## 

2\. Guacamole and Crackers

Guacamole is a vegan dip usually made from [avocado](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/12-proven-benefits-of-avocado), onion, garlic and lime juice.

It is very healthy and contains many beneficial nutrients. For example, avocados are an excellent source of [monounsaturated fats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/monounsaturated-fats), fiber and potassium — all of which may promote heart health ([4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3664913/), [5](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/1843/2)).

You can prepare your own guacamole or purchase a premade version without added salt or sugar. Choose 100% whole-grain crackers to pair with guacamole for a healthy vegan snack.

## 

3\. Edamame With Sea Salt

Edamame is the name for immature soybeans in their pod.

They’re an excellent source of high-quality plant protein. One cup (155 grams) provides close to 17 grams of protein for less than 200 calories ([6](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5793271/), [7](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/vegetables-and-vegetable-products/9873/2)).

You can prepare [edamame](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/edamame-benefits) by boiling or steaming the pods or by thawing them in your microwave. Sprinkle the warm pods with a little [sea salt](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/different-types-of-salt) or soy sauce before chewing on them gently to eat the beans inside.

## 

4\. Trail Mix

Trail mix is a plant-based snack that typically includes nuts, seeds and dried fruit. Some varieties also have chocolate, coconut, crackers or [whole grains](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-benefits-of-whole-grains).

Depending on the ingredients, trail mix can be a good source of protein, healthy fats and fiber ([8](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/snacks/5377/2)).

However, some varieties may not be vegan or may contain [added sugar](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/too-much-sugar), salt and oil. To avoid these ingredients, you can easily make your own trail mix by combining your favorite plant-based ingredients.

## 

5\. Roasted Chickpeas

Chickpeas, also known as garbanzo beans, are spherical and slightly yellow legumes.

One cup (164 grams) of [chickpeas](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/chickpeas-nutrition-benefits) provides over 14 grams of protein and 71% of the daily value (DV) for folate. They’re also high in iron, copper, [manganese](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/manganese-benefits), phosphorus and magnesium ([9](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4326/2)).

Roasted chickpeas are a delicious vegan snack. You can make your own by tossing canned chickpeas in olive oil and seasonings, spreading them on a baking sheet and baking them for 40 minutes or until crunchy at 450°F (230°C).

## 

6\. Fruit Leather

[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fvegan-snacks&media=https%3A%2F%2Fimages-prod.healthline.com%2Fhlcmsresource%2Fimages%2FAN_images%2Fvegan-snacks-1296x728-fruit-leather.jpg&description=24%20Healthy%20Vegan%20Snack%20Ideas "Share on Pinterest")

Fruit leather is made from fruit puree that has been thinly flattened, dried and sliced.

It has similar nutrients to the fresh fruit from which it is made and is usually high in [fiber](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-is-fiber-good-for-you), vitamins and minerals. However, some packaged fruit leathers have added sugar or color and are not as nutritious as homemade varieties ([10](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/sweets/5342/2)).

To make your own, puree fruits of your choice and mix with lemon juice and [maple syrup](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/maple-syrup) if preferred. Spread the puree in a thin layer on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper and dry it in a dehydrator or in your oven at 140°F (60°C) for approximately six hours.

## 

7\. Rice Cakes and Avocado

Rice cakes are a snack food similar to crackers. They’re made from puffed rice that has been packed together and shaped into circles.

The most nutritious rice cakes are made from whole-grain [brown rice](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-brown-rice-good-for-you) and contain few other ingredients. Two brown rice cakes provide 14 grams of carbs for less than 70 calories ([11](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/snacks/5371/2)).

Rice cakes topped with avocado is a balanced vegan snack with both healthy fats and fiber. You can sprinkle the rice cakes with toasted sesame seeds for extra crunch and flavor.

## 

8\. Hummus and Veggies

[Hummus](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-hummus-healthy) is a vegan dip made from chickpeas, oil, lemon juice, garlic and a sesame seed paste called tahini.

It is high in fiber, healthy fats, B vitamins and vitamin C. Homemade versions are generally more nutritious than commercially prepared hummus that may have added vegetable oils and preservatives ([12](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4403/2), [13](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4407/2)).

You can pair homemade or store-bought hummus with carrot, celery, cucumber, radishes and other raw vegetables for a healthy and crunchy vegan snack.

## 

9\. Fruit and Veggie Smoothies

Smoothies are an excellent on-the-go snack for vegans.

Popular smoothie ingredients include fruits and veggies, which are rich in vitamins and minerals. You can easily make your own smoothie by blending [plant-based milk](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-milk-substitutes) or water with your favorite fruits and vegetables, including bananas, berries, spinach and kale.

If you follow a vegan diet, consider adding a scoop of flax or [chia seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-health-benefits-of-chia-seeds) that provide important omega-3 fatty acids that some vegan diets lack ([14](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/nut-and-seed-products/3061/2), [15](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25369925)).

## 

10\. Oatmeal With Fruit, Nuts or Seeds

Oatmeal is made by heating oats with liquid. It’s commonly eaten as a breakfast food but can be enjoyed at any time of the day for a quick and healthy vegan snack.

It’s high in fiber, iron, magnesium and several other vitamins and minerals. Cooking oatmeal with unsweetened almond milk and adding sliced fruit and nuts or seeds can boost the nutrient content ([16](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/breakfast-cereals/7298/2)).

The healthiest way to prepare [oatmeal](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-benefits-oats-oatmeal) is to make your own or choose instant options without added sugars or salt.

## 

11\. Salsa and Homemade Tortilla Chips

Salsa is typically made from chopped tomatoes, onions, lime juice, salt and seasonings.

It’s rich in vitamin C, potassium and the beneficial plant compound lycopene from the tomatoes. High intakes of [lycopene](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/lycopene) have been linked to a lower risk of heart disease ([17](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/soups-sauces-and-gravies/10679/2), [18](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3850026/)).

Salsa is commonly eaten with tortilla chips, but store-bought chips are often made with vegetable oil and excess salt. To make your own, simply slice a few tortillas, brush them with olive oil and bake for 15 minutes at 350°F (175°C).

## 

12\. Popcorn With Nutritional Yeast

[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fvegan-snacks&media=https%3A%2F%2Fimages-prod.healthline.com%2Fhlcmsresource%2Fimages%2FAN_images%2Fvegan-snacks-1296x728-nooch.jpg&description=24%20Healthy%20Vegan%20Snack%20Ideas "Share on Pinterest")

[Popcorn](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/popcorn-nutrition-and-health) is made by heating dried corn kernels. It can be prepared in an air popper, microwave or a kettle with oil on the stove.

When popcorn is made in an air popper, it can be a highly nutritious vegan snack. A two-cup serving (16 grams) has close to 10% of the DV for fiber at only 62 calories ([19](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/snacks/5356/2)).

Adding [nutritional yeast](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/nutritional-yeast) can boost the nutrition of popcorn even more. This flakey yellow yeast is a high-quality plant protein and usually fortified with zinc and B vitamins. It has a savory taste that some people compare to cheese ([20](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/custom/1323565/2)).

## 

13\. Homemade Granola

There are many types of granola, but most contain oats, [nuts](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/8-benefits-of-nuts) or seeds, dried fruits, spices and a sweetener.

Many store-bought granolas are laden with added sugar and vegetable oil. On the other hand, homemade varieties can be a healthy vegan snack rich in fiber, protein and healthy fats ([21](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/breakfast-cereals/1536/2)).

To make your own granola, combine old-fashioned oats, almonds, [pumpkin seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-benefits-of-pumpkin-seeds), raisins and cinnamon with melted coconut oil and maple syrup. Spread out the mixture on a lined baking sheet and bake for 30–40 minutes at low heat in your oven.

## 

14\. Fruit and Nut Bars

Fruit and nut bars are an easy on-the-go snack that can be very nutritious.

Brands that have vegan bar options include LaraBars, GoMacro Bars and KIND Bars. A Cashew Cookie LaraBar (48 grams) has five grams of protein, 6% of the DV for potassium and 8% of the DV for iron ([22](https://www.larabar.com/our-products/larabar/cashew-cookie)).

You can also make your own fruit and nut bars by combining 1–2 cups (125–250 grams) of nuts, one cup (175 grams) of [dried fruit](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/dried-fruit-good-or-bad) and 1/4 cup (85 grams) of maple or brown rice syrup.

Spread this mixture in a greased 8-inch (20-cm) baking pan and bake for approximately 20 minutes at 325°F (165°C).

## 

15\. White Bean Dip and Homemade Pita Chips

White bean dip is typically made by blending white or cannellini beans with olive oil, lemon juice, [garlic](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-health-benefits-of-garlic) and fresh herbs.

White beans have an impressive nutrient profile, packing approximately five grams of protein, over 10% of the DV for iron and four grams of fiber in just 1/4 cup (50 grams) ([23](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4320/2)).

Pairing pita chips with white bean dip makes for a healthy vegan snack. You can make homemade pita chips by slicing whole grain pitas, brushing them with olive oil and baking them for 10 minutes at 400°F (205°C).

## 

16\. Peanut Butter and Banana Bites

Peanut butter and [banana](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-benefits-of-bananas) is a popular and healthy snack combination.

Bananas are loaded with potassium and fiber, while peanut butter provides protein and healthy fats. Eating them together can keep you feeling full and satisfied ([1](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/1846/2), [24](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4453/2)).

To make peanut butter and banana bites, slice a banana into thin pieces and spread a layer of peanut butter in between two slices. These treats taste especially delicious when frozen for at least 30 minutes on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper in your freezer.

## 

17\. Dried Coconut and Dark Chocolate

For a healthy vegan snack that will also satisfy your sweet tooth, try eating dried coconut with a few squares of [dark chocolate](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/7-health-benefits-dark-chocolate).

Dried coconut is made from dehydrated coconut flakes or pieces. Unsweetened varieties are incredibly nutritious, packing 18% of the DV for fiber in just one ounce (28 grams) ([25](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/nut-and-seed-products/3107/2)).

As an added bonus, dark chocolate that is at least 65% cacao provides plant compounds and may have a number of health benefits. To make sure your dark chocolate is vegan, look for brands that do not contain any animal products ([26](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5465250/)).

## 

18\. Baked Veggie Chips

[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fvegan-snacks&media=https%3A%2F%2Fimages-prod.healthline.com%2Fhlcmsresource%2Fimages%2FAN_images%2Fvegan-snacks-1296x728-veggiechips.jpg&description=24%20Healthy%20Vegan%20Snack%20Ideas "Share on Pinterest")

Baked veggie chips made from sliced vegetables, dehydrated or baked at low temperatures, are a delicious vegan snack.

Depending on the type of vegetable, baked veggie chips provide a variety of nutrients. For example, dehydrated carrots are loaded with vitamin A while baked [beet](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/benefits-of-beets) chips are rich in potassium and folate ([27](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/vegetables-and-vegetable-products/7365/2), [28](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/vegetables-and-vegetable-products/2348/2)).

You can make your own vegetable chips by baking thinly sliced veggies at 200–250°F (90–120°C) for 30–60 minutes.

## 

19\. Spiced Nuts

Popular types of nuts include [almonds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-proven-benefits-of-almonds), pistachios, cashews, [walnuts](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/benefits-of-walnuts), macadamia nuts and pecans.

All nuts are an incredibly nutritious vegan snack option. For example, just one ounce (23 grams) of almonds has six grams of protein, over 12% of the DV for fiber and several vitamins and minerals ([29](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/nut-and-seed-products/3086/2)).

Nuts are especially delicious when coated in spices. You can buy spiced nuts at most grocery stores. To make homemade spiced nuts, toss your preferred variety in olive oil and seasonings before baking the mix for 15–20 minutes at 350°F (175°C).

## 

20\. Seaweed Crisps

[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fvegan-snacks&media=https%3A%2F%2Fimages-prod.healthline.com%2Fhlcmsresource%2Fimages%2FAN_images%2Fvegan-snacks-1296x728-seaweed.jpg&description=24%20Healthy%20Vegan%20Snack%20Ideas "Share on Pinterest")

[Seaweed](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/seaweed-healthy-nutritious) crisps are made from sheets of seaweed that have been baked, sliced into squares and seasoned with salt.

They’re a vegan, low-calorie snack loaded with folate (vitamin B9), fiber and vitamins A and C. Seaweed is also an excellent source of iodine, a nutrient that naturally occurs in seawater and is vital to proper thyroid functioning ([30](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/vegetables-and-vegetable-products/2618/2), [31](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18236692), [32](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3074887/)).

When purchasing seaweed crisps, look for varieties with minimal ingredients, such as SeaSnax, which only contains seaweed, olive oil and salt.

## 

21\. No-Bake Energy Balls

Energy balls refer to bite-size snacks that are typically made from a mix of oats, nuts, seeds, nut butter, dried fruit, maple syrup and occasionally chocolate chips or other add-ins.

Depending on their ingredients, they can be a very nutritious vegan snack with protein, fiber and healthy fats that promote energy and satiety ([14](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/nut-and-seed-products/3061/2), [24](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/legumes-and-legume-products/4453/2)).

To make homemade energy balls, you can combine one cup (90 grams) of old-fashioned oats, 1/2 cup (125 grams) of peanut butter, 1/3 cup (113 grams) maple syrup, two tablespoons of [hemp seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/6-health-benefits-of-hemp-seeds) and two tablespoons of raisins.

Divide and roll the batter into balls and store in your refrigerator.

## 

22\. Ants on a Log

Ants on a log is the name of a popular snack made from celery sticks stuffed with peanut butter and [raisins](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/raisins-sultanas-currants).

This vegan treat is rich in fiber from celery, healthy fats from peanut butter and vitamins and minerals from the raisins ([33](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/2050/2)).

To make ants on a log, simply slice a few stalks of celery into pieces, add peanut butter and sprinkle with raisins.

## 

23\. Almond-Butter-Stuffed Dried Dates

[Dates](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/benefits-of-dates) are chewy, brown fruits that grow on palm trees and have a sweet and nutty flavor.

They contain natural sugars and fiber that can give you a quick boost of energy. In fact, one date has approximately 18 grams of carbs ([34](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/7348/2)).

For a healthy vegan snack, you can remove the pits of dates and stuff them with almond butter. However, keep in mind that they are high in calories, so remember to watch your portion size.

## 

24\. Frozen Grapes

[Grapes](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/benefits-of-grapes) are small spherical fruits that grow on vines and come in purple, red, green and black.

One cup (151 grams) of grapes has 28% of the DV for vitamin K and 27% of the DV for vitamin C. They are also rich in polyphenols, which are plant compounds that may protect against heart disease and type 2 diabetes ([35](https://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/fruits-and-fruit-juices/1920/2), [36](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5601283/)).

Frozen grapes are a delicious vegan snack. For a refreshing treat, keep grapes in a container in your freezer and enjoy a handful when hunger strikes.

## 

The Bottom Line

If you’re following a [vegan diet](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vegan-diet-guide) — or are trying to reduce the number of animal foods you’re eating — it’s a good idea to keep plant-based snacks on hand.

The vegan snacks above are a great way to combat hunger between meals.

They’re easy to make and a nutritious option for vegans and those just looking to eat more plant foods.

# Is Hummus Healthy? 8 Great Reasons to Eat More Hummus

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Jared-Meacham-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Jared Meacham, PhD., RD, CSCS — Written by Ryan Raman, MS, RD — Updated on April 18, 2025

-   [Nutritional value](#nutritional-value)
-   [Inflammation](#inflammation)
-   [Digestive health](#digestive-health)
-   [Blood sugar](#blood-sugar)
-   [Heart health](#heart-health)
-   [Weight management](#weight-management)
-   [Allergy-friendly](#allergy-friendly)
-   [Takeaway](#takeaway)

Hummus provides key nutrients, including protein. The ingredients in hummus may benefit your overall health and reduce the risk of certain health conditions.

![fresh hummus with crackers](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/hummus-1296x728-header.jpg?w=1155&h=1528)[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fnutrition%2Fis-hummus-healthy&media=https%3A%2F%2Fpost.healthline.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2023%2F05%2Fhummus-1296x728-header.jpg&description=Is%20Hummus%20Healthy%3F%20Top%208%20Benefits%20of%20Hummus "Share on Pinterest")

Nataša Mandić/Stocksy United Photo

Hummus is an incredibly popular Middle Eastern dip and spread.

It is typically made by blending chickpeas (garbanzo beans), tahini (ground sesame seeds), olive oil, lemon juice, and garlic in a food processor.

Not only is hummus delicious, but it is also versatile, packed with nutrients, and has been [linked](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0963996924005878) to many impressive health and nutritional benefits.

Here are 8 scientifically proven benefits of hummus.

## 

1\. Highly nutritious

Hummus contains a wide variety of vitamins and minerals.

A 2-tablespoon (tbsp) serving of hummus [contains](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/321358/nutrients):

-   **Calories:** 82
-   **Fat:** 5.8 g
-   **Protein:** 2.5 g
-   **Carbs:** 5 g
-   **Fiber:** 2 g
-   **Manganese:** 0.4 mg
-   **Copper:** 0.12 mg
-   **Magnesium:** 24.1 mg
-   **Phosphorus:** 56.3 mg
-   **Folate:** 12.2 mg
-   **Iron:** 0.82 mg
-   **Zinc:** 0.5 mg
-   **Thiamine:** 0.5 mg

Hummus contains a small amount of [plant-based protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/protein-for-vegans-vegetarians), with around 2 g in each 2-tbsp (30-g) serving.

In addition, hummus includes iron and [folate](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/folic-acid), both of which are important for vegetarians and vegans, as they [may not get enough](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8746448/) from their diet.

> **Summary**
> 
> Hummus provides a wide variety of vitamins and minerals, including manganese, copper, folate, and iron.

## 

2\. May help ease inflammation

Inflammation is the body’s way to protect itself from infection, illness, or injury.

However, sometimes inflammation can persist longer than necessary. This is called [chronic inflammation](https://www.healthline.com/health/chronic-inflammation), and it has been [linked](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK493173/) to many serious health problems.

Hummus is packed with healthy ingredients that may help reduce chronic inflammation.

Olive oil is one of them. It’s rich in powerful antioxidants that have [anti-inflammatory](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/13-anti-inflammatory-foods) benefits.

In particular, [extra virgin olive oil](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-benefits-of-olive-oil) contains the antioxidant [oleocanthal](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5986450/), which is [believed](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6950227/) to have similar anti-inflammatory properties as common anti-inflammatory medicines.

Similarly, [sesame seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/sesame-seeds), which make up tahini, may [help reduce](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8575626/) markers of inflammation in the body, such as [interleukin-6 (IL-6)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32734482/), which is elevated in inflammatory conditions like arthritis.

Moreover, research shown that consuming a diet rich in legumes like chickpeas [reduces inflammation](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/potential-antiinflammatory-effects-of-legumes-a-review/231998C3D6AF1AB14F2E722061E195C8).

> **Summary**
> 
> Hummus contains chickpeas, olive oil, and sesame seeds (tahini), which are proven to have anti-inflammatory properties.

## 

3\. Supports digestive health

Hummus is a great source of dietary fiber, which can improve digestive health.

It provides nearly 2 g of dietary fiber per 2-tbsp (30-g) serving, which is about 6% of the [DV for fiber](https://www.fda.gov/food/nutrition-facts-label/daily-value-nutrition-and-supplement-facts-labels).

Thanks to its fiber content, hummus may help keep you regular. This is because dietary fiber [helps](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7589116/) soften and add bulk to stools so that they are easier to pass.

What’s more, dietary fiber also helps feed the [healthy bacteria](https://www.healthline.com/health/gut-health) that live in your gut.

Some of the fiber in hummus may also be converted by gut bacteria into the [short-chain fatty acid](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/short-chain-fatty-acids-101) butyrate. This fatty acid helps [nourish the colon cells](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29866614/) and has many impressive benefits.

Laboratory [studies](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29866614/) have shown that butyrate reduces inflammation in the intestines and may help protect against colon cancer.

> **Summary**
> 
> Hummus is a great source of fiber, which support regularity. Additionally, chickpea fiber may promote the growth of healthy gut bacteria, which produce butyrate — a type of fatty acid that helps nourish cells in the gut.

## 

4\. May help manage blood sugar levels

Hummus has several properties that may help manage your blood sugar levels.

First, hummus is made mostly from chickpeas, which have a [low glycemic index (GI)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34258626/).

The [glycemic index](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/glycemic-index) is a scale that measures the ability of foods to raise [blood sugar](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/15-ways-to-lower-blood-sugar).

Foods with a high GI value are quickly digested and then absorbed, causing a sharp spike and fall in blood sugar levels. Conversely, foods with a low GI value are slowly digested and then absorbed, causing a slower and more balanced rise and fall in blood sugar levels.

Hummus is also a great source of soluble fiber and [healthy fats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-super-healthy-high-fat-foods). Fats help slow down the absorption of carbs from the gut, which, in turn, provides a slower and more steady release of sugar into the bloodstream.

> **Summary**
> 
> Hummus has a low glycemic index, which means it slowly releases sugar into the bloodstream. This is also aided by the resistant starch, fat, and protein it contains.

## 

5\. Promotes heart health

Heart disease is the [leading cause of](https://www.who.int/health-topics/cardiovascular-diseases#tab=tab_1) death around the globe, affecting around 17.9 million people each year.

Hummus contains several ingredients that may help [reduce risk factors](https://www.cmbr-journal.com/article_175511.html) for [heart disease](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/heart-healthy-foods).

Aside from chickpeas, hummus is also a great source of heart-healthy fats from olive oil.

According to one [analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35027106/) of over 92,000 people, replacing margarine, butter, mayonnaise, and dairy fat with an equal amount of olive oil was associated with an 8%–34% lower risk of total and cause-specific death.

While these results are promising, more long-term studies on the effects of hummus specifically are needed.

> **Summary**
> 
> Hummus contains chickpeas and olive oil — two ingredients that may reduce risk factors, and thus overall risk, for heart disease.

## 

6\. Supports weight management

[Research from 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/12/12/3678) found that incorporating hummus into your diet may support weight loss goals by promoting better appetite control and reducing overall daily food intake compared to other commonly consumed foods.

The nutrient-rich combination of chickpeas and tahini offers benefits such as improved postprandial glycemic control and healthier fasting lipid levels, which are important factors in managing weight and metabolic health.

Additionally, hummus can enhance your overall diet quality by replacing less nutritious options that are high in saturated fat, sodium, or added sugars.

These properties make hummus a versatile and healthful addition to dietary patterns that aim to reduce obesity and support long-term weight management.

> **Summary**
> 
> Research has found that adding hummus to your diet can support weight loss by improving appetite control, enhancing diet quality, and helping replace less nutritious foods.

## 

7\. Free of gluten, nuts, and dairy

Food allergies and intolerances [affect](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30646188/) many people in the United States.

People with [food allergies](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/common-food-allergies) and intolerances may have difficulty finding foods they can eat that won’t cause uncomfortable symptoms.

Hummus is naturally free of gluten, nuts, and dairy, which makes it suitable for people with conditions like celiac disease, nut allergies, and lactose intolerance.

Although hummus is naturally free of these ingredients, it’s still a good idea to read the full list of ingredients, as some brands may add preservatives or other ingredients.

Additionally, note that chickpeas are high in raffinose, a type of carbohydrate known as a [FODMAP](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/fodmaps-101). People who are sensitive to FODMAPs, such as those with irritable bowel syndrome, should [be careful](https://ift.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1541-4337.12903) not to overindulge in hummus.

## 

Takeaway

Hummus is a versatile ingredient and a good source of fiber, along with vitamins and minerals like manganese, copper, folate, and iron.

Thanks to its impressive nutrient profile, it may help ease inflammation, promote heart health, and support blood sugar management.

It’s also naturally free of gluten, nuts, and dairy and can be enjoyed as a delicious dip, spread, or condiment in a variety of recipes.

# Is Nutritional Yeast Healthy? All You Need to Know

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Katherine-Marengo-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Katherine Marengo LDN, R.D. — Written by Lauren Panoff, MPH, RD and Erica Julson, MS, RDN, CLT — Updated on March 8, 2023

-   [Basics](#basics)
-   [Nutrients and benefits](#nutrients-and-benefits)
-   [Vitamin B12](#vitamin-b-12)
-   [Antioxidants](#antioxidants)
-   [Immunity](#immunity)
-   [Cholesterol](#cholesterol)
-   [Uses](#uses)
-   [Safety](#safety)
-   [Bottom line](#bottom-line)

Nutritional yeast is an inactivated version of the yeast you’d use to make bread or beer. It has a savory or umami flavor, and may help lower cholesterol, boost your immune health, and more.

Nutritional yeast, also called “nooch,” is a popular food product often used in vegan cooking.

This powdered yeast gets its name from the nutrients it contains, including protein and a wide array of vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants.

Studies show that nutritional yeast offers several potential health benefits, ranging from lower cholesterol to protection from cellular damage that leads to disease.

This article explains what nutritional yeast is, reviews its health benefits, and suggests creative ways to use it.

## 

What is nutritional yeast?

Nutritional yeast (sometimes referred to as “nooch”) is a species of yeast known as *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, which is the same type of yeast that’s used to bake bread and brew beer ([1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/pmc/articles/PMC7099199/)).

However, nutritional yeast is an inactivated version, which means that, if you tried to use it to make bread or beer, it wouldn’t work well and would likely be too bitter to enjoy.

### Comparison with other types of yeast

The three main types of yeast that come from *S. cerevisiae* are:

-   **Baker’s yeast.** Baker’s yeast is used to leaven bread. The yeast is killed during cooking but imparts an earthy, yeasty flavor.
-   **Brewer’s yeast.** [Brewer’s yeast](https://www.healthline.com/health/brewers-yeast) is used to brew beer. The dead yeast cells left over from the brewing process can be taken as a nutritional supplement, but they taste very bitter.
-   **Nutritional yeast.** This version is grown specifically for use as a food product. The yeast cells are killed during manufacturing, which makes this yeast inactive. It’s used in cooking or as a seasoning, offering a savory or [umami flavor](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/umami-foods).

### Types of nutritional yeast

To produce nutritional yeast, *S. cerevisiae* cells are grown for several days on a sugar-rich medium, like molasses. The yeast is then deactivated with heat, harvested, washed, dried, crumbled, and packaged for distribution.

There are two types of nutritional yeast — unfortified and fortified:

-   **Unfortified.** This type doesn’t provide any added vitamins or minerals. It only contains the vitamins and minerals that are naturally produced by the yeast cells as they grow.
-   **Fortified.** This type contains synthetic vitamins added during the manufacturing process to boost nutrient content. Any additional vitamins are included in the ingredient list.

Fortified nutritional yeast is the most common type and offers the most benefits.

Nutritional yeast is sold as thin flakes, granules, or powder. You can find it in the spice or [condiment](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/list-of-condiments) section at most grocery stores or in bulk bins of health food stores. It has a pale yellow color and may come packaged in a bag, shaker, or plastic container.

This ingredient fits nearly any diet or eating style. It’s naturally low in sodium and calories, and it’s fat-free, sugar-free, gluten-free, and [vegan](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vegan-diet-guide).

> **Summary**
> 
> Nutritional yeast is a vegan food product with a savory, umami flavor. It comes in both fortified and unfortified varieties.

## 

Nutrients and health benefits of nutritional yeast

Nutritional yeast is a great source of [plant-based protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/protein-for-vegans-vegetarians), B vitamins, and trace minerals.

Fortified nutritional yeast contains more [B vitamins](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-b-complex) than unfortified varieties, as extra amounts are added during manufacturing.

Just 2 teaspoons (5 grams) of fortified nutritional yeast contain ([2](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/2194488/nutrients)):

-   **Calories:** 20
-   **Protein:** 3 grams
-   **Fat:** 0 grams
-   **Carbs:** 2 grams
-   **Sugar:** 0 grams
-   **Fiber:** 4% of the Daily Value (DV)
-   **Riboflavin (vitamin B2):** 246% of the DV
-   **Niacin (vitamin B3):** 109% of the DV
-   **Vitamin B6:** 212% of the DV
-   **Folate (vitamin B9):** 59% of the DV
-   **Vitamin B12:** 313% of the DV
-   **Iron:** 2% of the DV
-   **Potassium:** 2% of the DV

### Nutrients of note

Some main nutritional benefits of nutritional yeast include:

-   **Protein.** Nutritional yeast contains all nine [essential amino acids](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/essential-amino-acids) that you must obtain from food. It’s also a source of high quality plant protein ([2](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/2194488/nutrients)).
-   **B vitamins.** Fortified nutritional yeast is especially rich in B vitamins, including thiamine (B1), riboflavin (B2), niacin (B3), and B6 and B12 ([2](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/2194488/nutrients)).
-   **Trace minerals.** These include zinc, selenium, manganese, and molybdenum, which are involved in gene regulation, metabolism, growth, and immunity ([2](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/2194488/nutrients), [3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30202269/), [4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31752257/)).

Exact nutritional values vary between brands, so always [read labels](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-read-food-labels) to find the variety that meets your needs.

Buy fortified versions to get the most health benefits, particularly if you’re using nooch to add extra vitamins and minerals to your diet. If you’re using nutritional yeast simply for its flavor, you may be less concerned about whether it’s fortified.

> **Summary**
> 
> Fortified nutritional yeast is a vegan-friendly source of protein, B vitamins, and trace minerals that support optimal health.

## 

Packed with vitamin B12

One of the [biggest nutritional concerns](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vegetarian-and-vegan-mistakes) for people following a vegan diet, which excludes all animal products, is getting enough vitamin B12 ([5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/pmc/articles/PMC5188422/), [6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31752105/)).

This vitamin is essential for keeping your blood and nerve cells healthy. It also helps make DNA and prevent megaloblastic anemia, a blood condition that makes you feel weak and fatigued ([5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/pmc/articles/PMC5188422/), [6](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31752105/)).

The most reliable and consistent source of vitamin B12 on a vegan diet is a supplement. However, eating foods fortified with this vitamin, such as nutritional yeast, may also help.

Notably, a mere 2 teaspoons of nutritional yeast packs a whopping 313% of the DV for [vitamin B12](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-b12-foods) ([2](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/fdc-app.html#/food-details/2194488/nutrients)).

> **Summary**
> 
> Fortified nutritional yeast is a very rich source of vitamin B12 — an essential nutrient that you should try to get enough of, especially if you follow a vegan diet.

## 

Contains powerful antioxidants

Antioxidants are compounds which, when consumed, fight unstable molecules called free radicals that may increase your risk of disease ([7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29952268/), [8](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30055541/)).

Studies reveal that nutritional yeast contains the powerful antioxidants [glutathione](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-increase-glutathione) and selenomethionine ([9](https://bmccomplementmedtherapies.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12906-017-1591-9), [10](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29359222/)).

These compounds may help protect your cells from damage caused by free radicals and heavy metals and help your body eliminate environmental toxins ([11](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33871746/), [12](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30261313/)).

Eating [antioxidant-rich foods](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods-high-in-antioxidants), like nutritional yeast, may also reduce your risk of chronic ailments, including ([13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27881064/)):

-   heart disease
-   cancer
-   macular degeneration

> **Summary**
> 
> Nutritional yeast contains the antioxidants glutathione and selenomethionine, which may protect your body from chronic diseases.

## 

May boost immune health

Nutritional yeast contains two main carbs: alpha mannan and [beta glucan](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/beta-glucan-heart-healthy).

Animal studies suggest these carbs offer antibacterial and antifungal benefits, which may safeguard your body from infections ([14](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32433977/), [15](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26597205/), [16](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29401667/), [17](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25740827/)).

In particular, beta glucan may work by activating immune cells and targeting the [gut microbiome](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/gut-microbiome-and-health) to support immunity and overall health ([18](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/pmc/articles/PMC3202617/), [19](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30196242/)).

Still, human research is necessary.

> **Summary**
> 
> Nutritional yeast contains the carbs alpha mannan and beta glucan, which studies suggest may boost immunity.

## 

May help lower cholesterol levels

The beta glucan in nutritional yeast may also help [lower cholesterol](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/13-foods-that-lower-cholesterol-levels).

In an 8-week study, men with high cholesterol who took 15 grams of yeast derived beta glucan daily lowered their total cholesterol levels by 6% ([20](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28872611)).

Beta glucan is found in other foods, such as oats and [barley](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/barley-benefits).

Extensive research shows that the beta glucan from oats can significantly lower cholesterol levels, high levels of which are a risk factor for heart disease ([21](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29393350/), [22](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31960663/), [23](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27724985/), [24](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27273067/)).

Although the chemical structure of beta glucan in oats differs slightly from that of this carb in yeast, older research suggests they have similar cholesterol-lowering effects ([25](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/pmc/articles/PMC3832763/)).

However, particular studies on nutritional yeast are lacking.

> **Summary**
> 
> The beta glucan in nutritional yeast may help lower cholesterol levels, which may benefit heart health.

## 

How to use nutritional yeast

You should keep nutritional yeast in a cool, dark place to preserve its vitamin content. Furthermore, seal the container tightly to keep moisture out. When properly stored, it can last up to 2 years.

Here are a few uses for nooch:

-   as a seasoning for popcorn, pasta, [salad](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthy-salad-toppings), or casserole dishes
-   as an umami flavor in soups, stews, or chili
-   as a savory, cheesy flavor in vegan sauces
-   as a thickener for soups and sauces
-   as an ingredient in [smoothies](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/are-smoothies-good-for-you)
-   as a pet food additive

Serving sizes for nutritional yeast depend on the recipe, but you typically use 2–4 teaspoons (5–10 grams).

> **Summary**
> 
> Nutritional yeast is shelf-stable for up to 2 years when stored properly. You can add it to many foods for a nutty, cheesy, or savory flavor, as well as extra vitamin and mineral content.

## 

Side effects and safety

It’s [safe to use](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/nutritional-yeast-dangers) nutritional yeast in moderation, typically up to several tablespoons (10–30 grams) per day.

It would require relatively large amounts of nutritional yeast to exceed the tolerable upper intake levels (UL) for the various vitamins and minerals it contains.

Still, studies suggest anyone who is [allergic](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/common-food-allergies) to yeast should avoid it ([26](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34661531/), [27](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33342171/)).

Those who have trouble metabolizing folic acid, such as people who have an MTHFR gene mutation, should read labels carefully and may want to choose unfortified nutritional yeast ([28](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25449138/)).

> **Summary**
> 
> Nutritional yeast is largely considered safe, though some people may be allergic. Those with a particular gene mutation may need to buy unfortified versions.

## 

The bottom line

Nutritional yeast is a highly nutritious vegan food product with many potential health benefits.

With it, you can easily add extra protein, vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants to meals. It’s commonly used as a vegan cheese sauce flavoring, as well as a topping for [soups](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-soup-healthy) and salads.

Studies suggest nutritional yeast may help lower cholesterol and [support immunity](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-boost-immune-health), though more research is needed.

### Just one thing

*Try this today:* Take advantage of nutritional yeast’s flavor and benefits by using it as a seasoning. Sprinkle it on top of mixed green salads, soups, or warm pasta dishes, like [pesto](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-pesto-healthy) noodles or spaghetti with marinara sauce.

# 30 High Protein Snacks That Are Healthy and Portable

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/Marie-Lorraine-Johnson_500x500-MR-1.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Marie Lorraine Johnson MS, RD, CPT — Written by Brianna Elliott, RD — Updated on April 15, 2025

Healthy protein-rich snacks can include almonds, trail mix, and Greek yogurt parfaits. They may help you feel full in between meals.

If you live a busy lifestyle, snacks can be useful when hunger hits, and you don’t have time to prepare a meal.

However, many snack foods available today are high in refined carbs and sugar, which can leave you feeling unsatisfied and craving more food.

The key is to make sure your snacks are nutritious and contain protein.

Protein promotes fullness because it [signals](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7539343) the release of appetite-suppressing hormones, slows digestion, and stabilizes blood sugar levels.

Many foods contain protein. Foods sourced from animals, including meat, seafood, eggs, and dairy, tend to be highest in protein.

Plant foods such as nuts, beans, and legumes contain varying amounts of protein. But if you follow a vegan or vegetarian diet, eating a variety of plant-based proteins throughout the day can help you meet your protein needs.

Plus, eating protein from various sources is good for your health. According to the U.S. Department of Agriculture, many people in the United States [would benefit](https://www.myplate.gov/eat-healthy/protein-foods) from shifting their protein intake to include more seafood and plant sources.

Here are 30 protein-loaded snacks that are healthy and portable so you can enjoy them even when you’re on the go.

## 

1\. Jerky

Jerky is meat that has been trimmed of fat, cut into strips, and dried. It makes an excellent and convenient snack.

It’s very high in [protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-much-protein-per-day/) — for example, beef jerky [contains](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/167536/nutrients) an impressive 9 grams (g) of protein per ounce (28 g).

Beef, chicken, turkey, and salmon are often made into jerky. You can find it at most grocery stores, but store-bought versions are typically high in [added sugar](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/sugar-the-worst-ingredient-in-the-diet/) and artificial ingredients.

You can also make jerky using only meat and some seasonings.

## 

2\. Trail mix

Trail mix consists of dried fruit and nuts and sometimes includes chocolate and grains. It’s a good source of protein, [providing](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2707576/nutrients) about 5.5 g in a 50-gram (g) serving.

You can increase the amount of protein in trail mix by using [almonds](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2346393/nutrients) or [pistachios](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2515379/nutrients), which are slightly higher in protein than other [nuts](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/8-benefits-of-nuts), such as [walnuts](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2346394/nutrients) or [cashews](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2515374/nutrients).

The dried fruit and nuts in trail mix make it very high in calories, so it’s important not to eat too much at a time. A handful is a reasonable serving.

## 

3\. Turkey roll-ups

Turkey roll-ups are a delicious and nutritious high protein snack consisting of cheese and veggies wrapped inside slices of turkey breast.

They’re like a sandwich without the [bread](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-is-bread-bad-for-you/).

Snacks high in protein and low in [carbs](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-many-carbs-per-day-to-lose-weight/), such as turkey roll-ups, [have been shown](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7956086/) to improve blood sugar levels, which is an important factor in appetite regulation.

You can make roll-ups by placing a slice of turkey breast on a plate, topping it with a slice of cheese, such as cheddar, and placing a pickle or strip of cucumber and a tomato slice on top. Then, roll everything into a wrap.

Each wrap provides about 6.5 g of protein from the [turkey](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/172941/nutrients) and [cheese](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/328637/nutrients) and some extra nutrients and fiber from the tomato and cucumber. You can increase the protein amount by adding more slices of turkey.

## 

4\. Greek yogurt parfait

Greek [yogurt](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods/yogurt/) is a healthy and high protein snack, with [16 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/330137/nutrients) of protein per container or 156 g serving. It has is more filling than yogurts with lower protein content.

In addition to being a great source of protein, Greek yogurt is high in [calcium](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-dairy-good-for-your-bones/), which is important for bone health.

To make yogurt even more delicious and filling, you can make a parfait by combining 1 cup of yogurt with granola and mixed berries in layers.

Adding 1/2 cup (61 g) of granola to your yogurt can add protein. However, this makes a large snack that’s high in calories, so depending on your overall calorie needs, you may not want to snack on this every day.

## 

5\. Veggies and yogurt dip

Veggies are great for [snacking](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/snacking-good-or-bad/) but not very high in protein. Pairing them with yogurt dip can increase your protein intake.

Yogurt dip is typically made by combining yogurt with herbs and flavorings, such as dill and lemon juice, as in this [recipe](http://www.thekitchn.com/recipe-herbed-yogurt-dip-recipes-from-the-kitchn-190686). For more protein, it’s best to use Greek yogurt, which contains almost twice as much protein as [regular yogurt](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2647437/nutrients).

A 100-g portion (1/3–1/2 cup) of Greek yogurt contains [10 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/330137/nutrients) of protein.

For convenience, you can make a batch of yogurt dip ahead of time and portion it into snack-size containers to grab it when you need it.

## 

6\. Tuna

Tuna is loaded with protein and makes a very healthy and convenient snack. One 171-g of canned tuna contains an impressive [50 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173708/nutrients) of protein, which makes it extra filling.

Additionally, tuna is high in various other nutrients, such as B vitamins and selenium, and contains a considerable amount of [omega-3 fatty acids](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/17-health-benefits-of-omega-3/).

## 

7\. Hard-boiled eggs

[Eggs](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-proven-health-benefits-of-eggs/) are undeniably healthy, containing almost every nutrient your body needs. They’re particularly [high in](https://www.egginfo.co.uk/egg-nutrition-and-health/egg-nutrition-information/vitamins-and-minerals) B vitamins and trace minerals.

In addition to being nutritious, they’re versatile. Hard-boiled eggs make a great portable snack.

One hard-boiled egg provides almost [4 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/747997/nutrients) of protein, which will keep you full and satisfied until your next meal. Eggs’ fullness-promoting properties [may also reduce](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7432073/) the calories you consume later in the day.

## 

8\. Peanut butter celery sticks

Celery sticks spread with 1 to 2 tablespoons (tbsp.) of peanut butter make a delicious and easy snack. They contain a decent amount of protein from the peanut butter, which provides [7 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2262072/nutrients) of protein per 2-tbsp. (30-g) serving.

[Peanut butter](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-peanut-butter-bad-for-you/) and peanuts may help you feel full and promote feelings of fullness when consumed between meals.

## 

9\. No-bake energy bites

Energy bites are a delicious snack made by combining various ingredients, such as nut butter, oats, and seeds, and then rolling them into balls.

The best part about energy bites is that they don’t require baking. You can make a batch ahead of time to have a snack available when you need to grab one and go.

If you include protein powder in your diet, consider trying a recipe that uses it. For instance, [pumpkin spice energy balls](https://www.healthline.com/recipes/pumpkin-spice-bites) with protein powder, dates, coconut, and flaxseed meal provide 9 g of protein per serving.

[Matcha superfood energy bites](https://www.healthline.com/recipes/matcha-superfood-energy-bites) containing pecans, matcha powder, protein powder, and dates contain about 4 g of protein per bite.

## 

10\. Slice of cheese

In addition to being a quick and easy snack, [cheese](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods/cheese/) is incredibly healthy and filling. It’s an excellent source of calcium, phosphorus, and selenium, and it contains small amounts of many other nutrients.

Furthermore, cheese is rich in protein. Just one slice 17-g slice of cheddar cheese provides [about 4 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/328637/nutrients) of this nutrient, which [may help](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31039320/) you feel less hungry.

A reasonable portion size for cheese is around 1 to 2 oz (28 to 57 g). Since it contains a significant number of [calories](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-many-calories-per-day/), it’s best consumed in moderation.

## 

11\. Handful of almonds

Eating a handful of [almonds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-proven-benefits-of-almonds/) or another type of nut for a snack is a simple way to consume more protein.

One ounce of almonds (about 28 g) provides [6 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2346393/nutrients) of protein and good amounts of vitamin E, riboflavin, trace minerals, and healthy fats.

Almonds [are also associated](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34201139/) with other health benefits, including diversity of the gut microbiome and reduced risk factors for cardiovascular disease.

Almonds are also high in calories, so it’s important to stick with the recommended serving size. A handful is equivalent to around 22 almonds.

## 

12\. Roasted chickpeas

Chickpeas, or garbanzo beans, are a [legume](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/legumes-good-or-bad/) with an impressive nutrient profile and an excellent source of [fiber](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-is-fiber-good-for-you/).

As a source of plant-based protein, chickpeas are a great snacking option for those following a vegetarian or vegan diet.

One half-cup (82 g) of cooked chickpeas contains [7 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173757/nutrients) of protein and 6 g of fiber, and many vitamins and minerals. They’re particularly high in folate, iron, magnesium, phosphorus, copper, and manganese.

One tasty way to prepare chickpeas for a snack is to roast them with some basic seasonings and olive oil. Roasted chickpeas are crunchy and portable, so you can take them and enjoy them when hunger hits.

## 

13\. Baked tofu

Tofu is a rich source of protein. While it’s particularly known for being vegan- and vegetarian-friendly, [tofu](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/what-is-tofu) is a nutrient-rich protein source for any diet.

Tofu is [made from](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8306988/) soybeans that have been ground, cooked, and pressed to produce a solid curd.

A 3-oz (84-g) serving of firm tofu contains [9 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173788/nutrients) of protein, which makes it a filling snack.

Cubes of baked tofu are easy to pack up and enjoy on the go. You can try baking tofu that’s tossed in vegetable oil and seasoning for a crispy coating.

## 

14\. Cottage cheese

[Cottage cheese](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/cottage-cheese-is-super-healthy/) is known for being high in protein. It’s a filling snack that you can eat on the go.

A half-cup (113 g) of lowfat cottage cheese has [14 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173417/nutrients) of protein, which makes up 69% of its total calorie content.

Cottage cheese is also a good source of other important nutrients, including calcium, phosphorus, selenium, vitamin B12, and riboflavin.

You can enjoy cottage cheese on its own or combine it with fruits and nuts for a delicious snack.

## 

15\. Apple with peanut butter

[Apples](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods/apples/) and peanut butter taste great together and make for a nutrient-dense, high protein snack that provides many health benefits.

The fiber and [antioxidants](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/antioxidants-explained/) in apples [may](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10494637/) improve gut health and reduce the risk of heart disease, while peanut butter [may](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9011914/) increase HDL (good) cholesterol and lower LDL (bad) cholesterol and triglycerides.

Despite peanut butter’s positive effects on your health, it’s fairly high in calories, so it’s best consumed in moderation.

A snack of a [medium apple](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/171689/nutrients) with 2 tbsp. ([30 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2262072/nutrients)) peanut butter provides about 7.4 g of protein and some other nutrients, such as vitamin C and potassium.

## 

16\. Roasted watermelon seeds

When enjoying a slice of [watermelon](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods/watermelon), you might be in the habit of eating around the seeds.

Those seeds [contain](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7698065/) protein, zinc, and iron. You can roast them to make a crunchy, satisfying snack.

[Roasted watermelon seed kernels](https://www.healthline.com/health/food-nutrition/best-watermelon-seed-benefits) contain about [8 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/169407/nutrients) of protein per ounce (28 g).

You can roast watermelon seeds in the oven or buy them pre-roasted.

Watermelon seed butter is a spread you can purchase to replace nut butter in snacks or recipes.

## 

17\. Protein bars

Protein bars are an easy way to consume a significant amount of protein.

However, many store-bought versions are high in added sugar or sweeteners and other unnecessary ingredients.

[You can make a batch on your own](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/homemade-protein-bars-guide) by using seeds, dried fruit, and protein powder.

If you prefer to buy protein bars, consider choosing [healthier options](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-protein-bars) with minimal additives.

## 

18\. Canned salmon

Canned salmon is an excellent high protein snack you can take wherever you go. Just 3 oz (85 g) provides more than 19 g of protein and other nutrients, including niacin, vitamin B12, and selenium.

Salmon also provides anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acids, which [may](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/proceedings-of-the-nutrition-society/article/fish-n3-fatty-acids-cognition-and-dementia-risk-not-just-a-fishy-tale/E7DD7B2EE5188A0FDF9873AADDD3CAEE) lower your risk of heart disease, depression, and dementia.

You can eat canned salmon on its own, add some extra flavor with pepper, or pair it with crackers or chopped veggies.

Single-portion pouches of cooked salmon are available to make it easier to pack salmon with you on the go.

## 

19\. Chia pudding

[Chia](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-health-benefits-of-chia-seeds/) pudding has become a popular snack in recent years, and for good reason. In addition to being high in protein, it’s delicious and healthy.

There are [4 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/170554/nutrients) of protein in 1 oz of chia seeds, which provide some other nutrients, such as calcium, phosphorus, and manganese.

Moreover, they’re notable for their [high](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6994964/) omega-3 fatty acid content, which provides several health benefits.

For example, snacking on chia seeds [may help lower](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6994964/) triglyceride levels, which can reduce the risk of heart disease.

To make a protein-rich chia pudding, use cow’s milk or a high protein milk alternative such as [soy milk](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/soy-milk-ingredients). One cup (240 milliliters) of cow’s milk adds [8 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/746772/nutrients) of protein to your pudding, while soy milk adds about [7 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2257044/nutrients), depending on the brand.

Soak chia seeds in milk for a few hours until it reaches a pudding-like consistency. You can add flavorings such as [raspberry](https://www.healthline.com/recipes/raspberry-pudding) or [blueberry](https://www.healthline.com/recipes/blueberry-chia-pudding). For an extra treat, you can make the caramel topping included in the recipe or simply top off the pudding with some fruit.

## 

20\. Homemade granola

[Granola](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-granola-healthy) is a baked snack consisting of rolled oats, nuts, and a sweetener such as honey. Many store-bought granolas provide some protein, about [5 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2131337/nutrients) per 3/4-cup (57-g) portion. But they also tend to be very high in sugar and calories.

You can make your own version at home to get more protein and less sugar in your granola. All you have to do is bake oats, nuts, and seeds together. You can also add protein powder for an extra boost.

Although healthy in moderation, granola can be high in calories. Consider using granola in small amounts to garnish a cup of yogurt or berries.

## 

21\. Pumpkin seeds

[Pumpkin seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-benefits-of-pumpkin-seeds/) are great for a quick snack and contain protein and some other valuable nutrients.

One ounce (28 g) of pumpkin seeds contains [8.5](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/170556/nutrients) g of protein and significant amounts of fiber, magnesium, zinc, and polyunsaturated fatty acids. These seeds also provide beneficial antioxidants, including vitamin E and carotenoids.

Some [research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10030350/) suggests that eating pumpkin seeds may help prevent certain cancers, while their healthy fat content may benefit heart health.

Furthermore, their protein and fiber content makes them a great snack to curb hunger until you can eat a full meal. You can eat them raw, roast them with spices, or include them in a homemade [trail mix](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-trail-mix-healthy) or granola.

## 

22\. Nut butter

Nut butter is a good option for a quick and portable high protein snack.

In the United States, you can find single-serving nut butter packs in the nut butter section or checkout lanes of many grocery stores.

You can also scoop your preferred [peanut butter](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthiest-peanut-butter) or [almond butter](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-almond-butter) from the jar into small to-go containers.

Nut butters are quite nutrient-dense, providing a significant amount of healthy fats, B vitamins, vitamin E, magnesium, phosphorus, and trace minerals. A 2-tbsp. (30-g) serving of peanut butter contains [7 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2262072/nutrients) of protein.

## 

23\. Protein shakes

While it’s usually recommended to get your protein from whole food sources, [protein shakes](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/protein-shakes-weight-loss/) make for an easy snack that will add protein and other nutrients to your diet.

You can make these with [several types of protein powder](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-protein-powder/), including whey, egg white, soy, and pea protein.

[Whey protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/whey-protein-101/), in particular, [may be](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6412413/) beneficial for fullness.

Generally, a scoop of [whey](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173180/nutrients) or [soy](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/173181/nutrients) protein powder provides about 25 g of protein, which may help keep you full until your next meal.

To make a protein shake, you can simply blend 1 scoop of protein powder, 1 cup of water or milk, 1 cup of ice, and fruit if desired. Then, pour the mixture into a portable container so you can take it with you wherever you go.

## 

24\. Edamame

Edamame beans are immature [soybeans](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods/soybeans/) still in the pod. They’re high in protein, vitamins, and minerals and make for a quick and easy snack.

One cup of edamame provides just about every nutrient you need, including [13 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/168410/nutrients) of protein and large amounts of vitamin K and folate.

Typically, edamame is served as a steamed dish. Many stores offer precooked and frozen edamame that need to be heated in a microwave. All you have to do is place the heated edamame in a portable container so you can enjoy it on the go.

To enhance the flavor of edamame, add spices and seasonings of your choice.

## 

25\. Canned sardines

Canned [sardines](https://www.healthline.com/health/food-nutrition/are-sardines-good-for-you) can be a convenient and nutritious snack.

These small fish are rich in protein, calcium, and other nutrients. One Pacific sardine (38 g) canned in tomato sauce contains [8 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/175140/nutrients) protein.

The omega-3 fatty acids found in sardines [may help](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8780033/) protect your cardiovascular health and reduce inflammation in your body.

People describe their flavor as salty and slightly fishier than tuna. Many people enjoy them straight from the can, on a cracker, or in [Mediterranean-style cooking](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/anchovies-vs-sardines).

## 

26\. Quinoa

Quinoa is a [grain-like food](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/what-is-quinoa) that’s gluten-free and a [good source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7074363/) of protein, vitamins, and minerals.

One cup of cooked quinoa contains [8 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/168917/nutrients) of protein.

The chewy, nutty grains can be enjoyed as a side dish like rice. You can also add a scoop of quinoa to a salad of greens and chopped vegetables.

For a quick snack, try eating cooked quinoa like a hot cereal, topped with cinnamon and a drizzle of honey.

## 

27\. Lentil salad

A lentil salad is a great snack. It’s highly nutritious and a great [plant-based source of protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/protein-for-vegans-vegetarians/). In fact, 1 cup of cooked lentils provides [18 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/172421/nutrients) of protein, along with large amounts of iron, folate, and manganese.

In addition, lentils provide more than 50% of your recommended daily fiber intake. The specific [type of fiber](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/different-types-of-fiber/) found in lentils may promote a healthy gut, as it [helps feed](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9100130/) the good bacteria in your colon.

The combination of protein, fiber, and carbohydrates in lentils is especially helpful for promoting fullness. Consuming them regularly [may help](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9100130/) manage diabetes and reduce the risk of heart disease and some types of cancer.

To make a lentil salad, combine cooked lentils with chopped veggies, spices, and a dressing of your choice. To add flavor, top with balsamic vinegar and olive oil.

## 

28\. Overnight oats

Overnight [oatmeal](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-benefits-oats-oatmeal/) is easy to make, portable, and very nutritious.

Oats contain many vitamins and minerals. In addition, a 1/2-cup (40-g) serving provides about [13%](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2485431/nutrients) of your recommended daily fiber intake.

Oats have been shown to promote fullness in [several studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9930024/). This is likely due to their fiber content.

However, oats are not very high in protein on their own. One-half cup of oats contains about [5 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2485431/nutrients) of protein.

To turn overnight oats into a protein-rich snack, you can add protein add-ins such as cow’s milk or soy milk, Greek yogurt, nut butter, and protein powder.

An overnight oats [recipe](https://www.bakeandbacon.com/high-protein-overnight-oats-3-ways/) with cow’s milk, peanut butter, and protein powder boasts 20 g of protein per serving. To make a snack-size version, portion the overnight oats mixture into several small containers before chilling it overnight.

## 

29\. Egg bites

[Egg](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-proven-health-benefits-of-eggs/) bites are a super healthy snack with lots of protein.

You can make them by mixing eggs with veggies and seasonings, pouring the mixture into a muffin tin, and then baking it.

They’re also very convenient, as they can be eaten hot or cold. You can increase their nutrient content by making them with veggies and add more protein by topping them with 1–2 tbsp. of cheese.

Some mix-ins to try include spinach, onions, and bell peppers.

## 

30\. “Cheesy” seasoned popcorn

[Popcorn](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/popcorn-nutrition-and-health/) is a popular and healthy snack that provides B vitamins, magnesium, phosphorus, zinc, and manganese. It also contains a significant amount of fiber, with [4 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/170246/nutrients) per ounce (28 g).

In addition, some research suggests that popcorn is a particularly filling snack. In a [small 2012 study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3502142/), participants who snacked on popcorn were less hungry and ate less than those who snacked on potato chips.

Despite popcorn’s filling effects, it’s not incredibly high in protein. You can significantly increase its protein content by adding nutritional yeast, which provides [3 g](https://fdc.nal.usda.gov/food-details/2411476/nutrients) of protein per 2-tbsp. serving.

In addition to being a vegan-friendly source of protein, nutritional yeast adds an appealing cheese-like flavor. To enjoy “cheesy” popcorn as a snack, simply combine 3 cups of popcorn with 2 tbsp. of nutritional yeast and a pinch of salt.
