# How to Safely Lose Weight During Pregnancy

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/Med-Network-Headshots-500x500-Priya-HL-Blue@3x.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Priya Patel, MD — Written by Kristeen Cherney and Alina Sharon — Updated on June 20, 2025

-   [Is it healthy?](#is-it-healthy)
-   [Healthy pregnancy weight](#healthy-pregnancy-weight)
-   [Best weight loss approach](#best-weight-loss-approach)
-   [FAQ](#faq)
-   [Takeaway](#takeaway)

While it is possible to lose weight safely while pregnant through daily exercise and smaller portions, it isn’t necessarily a good idea. Most pregnant people need to gain weight, although those with higher BMIs should gain less.

Pregnancy, while exciting, may lead to a [weight dilemma](https://www.healthline.com/health-news/gaining-too-much-weight-during-pregnancy) if you have overweight. This is because of the inevitable weight gain associated with pregnancy.

Only your doctor can tell you what approach to weight, nutrition, and exercise is best for you and your baby.

But there are some general ways you can lead a healthy lifestyle during pregnancy to help ensure an optimal weight for yourself and your baby.

## 

Is it healthy to lose weight during pregnancy?

Most research on weight during pregnancy suggests that weight maintenance is usually the best approach, especially if you have overweight.

For those who have obesity, weight gain beyond what is expected during pregnancy [can increase](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6887834/) the chances of complications such as [high blood pressure](https://www.healthline.com/health/what-considered-high-blood-pressure/), [preeclampsia](https://www.healthline.com/health/preeclampsia/), [preterm labor](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/preterm-labor-adjunctive-therapy), and [gestational diabetes](https://www.healthline.com/health/gestational-diabetes).

However, weight loss during pregnancy is also associated with a chance of delivering a baby with [low birth weight](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/diet-nutrition). For this reason, maintaining your weight might be the safest approach.

In one [2017 review](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6887834/), researchers concluded that [eating a healthy diet](https://www.healthline.com/health/balanced-diet/) and [exercising](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/exercise-during-pregnancy/) can reduce weight gain during pregnancy and lower the chances of needing a [cesarean](https://www.healthline.com/health/c-section/) delivery.

## 

What weight is healthy during pregnancy?

It is typical to gain weight naturally during your pregnancy, with most of this weight gain happening in the [second](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/second-trimester-weight-gain) and [third trimesters](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/third-trimester-weight-gain). Your baby also grows rapidly during the last 2 months of pregnancy.

However, gaining weight beyond the expected amount can negatively affect your health and your baby’s development.

Having obesity while pregnant may contribute to complications such as [premature birth](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/premature-infant), [preeclampsia](https://www.healthline.com/health/preeclampsia), and blood clots.

Since [you will gain some weight](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/weight-gain-physical-changes) during pregnancy, it’s important to know how much is healthy. Aim to follow these [pregnancy weight gain](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/how-much-weight-should-you-gain-during-pregnancy/) guidelines from the [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)](https://www.cdc.gov/maternal-infant-health/pregnancy-weight/?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/maternalinfanthealth/pregnancy-weight-gain.htm) based on your body mass index (BMI) before your pregnancy.

For those who have obesity at the start of pregnancy, the [CDC](https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/pdfs/maternal-infant-health/pregnancy-weight-gain/tracker/single/obese_weight_tracker_508tagged.pdf) offers a guide to help with tracking pregnancy weight gain.

If your weight falls outside this range, you can make a consistent and gradual plan, focusing on [healthy lifestyle habits](https://www.healthline.com/health/best-healthy-living-blogs).

Here’s how to do so safely during pregnancy:

## 

Healthy lifestyle during pregnancy

### 1\. Cut down on calories

The first thing that can help is to [manage your daily calorie intake](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-many-calories-per-day). Eating more calories than you burn is the most common cause of weight gain.

If you had a suitable weight for your height before pregnancy, you will likely need [2,200 to 2,900](https://www.eatright.org/health/pregnancy/prenatal-nutrition/healthy-weight-during-pregnancy) calories per day during pregnancy, but this will vary by trimester:

-   **Trimester 1:** no additional calories
-   **Trimester 2:** an extra 340 calories per day
-   **Trimester 3:** an extra 450 calories per day than when you were not pregnant

Consuming fewer calories can help you lose weight, but be sure to speak with a healthcare professional before trying this.

You can start by [keeping a log](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/5-best-calorie-counters) of how many calories you usually eat. Then, talk with a registered dietitian about how much you can safely cut and which [food plans](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/pregnancy-diet-101) will help.

[Nutritional labels](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-read-food-labels) for foods in stores and restaurants can give you an idea of how many calories are in each food.

If you usually consume far more calories than this, consider cutting down gradually. For example, you can consider:

-   eating [smaller portions](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/8-tips-to-reduce-portions)
-   skipping condiments
-   swapping [less-healthy fats](https://www.healthline.com/health/heart-disease/good-fats-vs-bad-fats) such as butter for healthier [plant-based versions](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-super-healthy-high-fat-foods) such as olive oil
-   snacking on [fruit](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/best-fruits-to-eat)
-   eating more [vegetables](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/14-healthiest-vegetables-on-earth) and fewer starchy side dishes with your meals
-   drinking [plenty of water](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/7-health-benefits-of-water) and cutting out soda
-   [avoiding](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-foods-to-avoid-during-pregnancy) consuming large amounts of highly processed snacks such as chips and candy

Taking a [daily prenatal vitamin](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/best-prenatal-vitamins/) is essential to ensure that you’re getting all the nutrients you need. [Folate](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/folic-acid-vs-folate) is especially important because it helps decrease the chance of [developmental irregularities](https://www.healthline.com/health/birth-defects) in your baby.

And even if you’re cutting calories, it’s essential to consume enough to provide energy and [nutrients](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/nutrition) for yourself and your growing baby.

### 2\. Exercise 30 minutes daily

Some people may be afraid to exercise during pregnancy, assuming that it may harm their babies. But this definitely isn’t true. While some exercises, such as situps, can possibly be harmful, [exercise overall is extremely beneficial](https://www.healthline.com/health/fitness/top-iphone-android-apps).

It can help you maintain your weight, reduce the chance of developmental issues, and even ease some of the aches and pains you might experience during pregnancy.

The current recommendation is the same as for those who are not pregnant: [30 to 60 minutes](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fendo.2018.00546/full) of activity per day, 3–5 days per week. If this is too much for you at first, consider breaking up the 30 minutes into shorter blocks of time throughout the day.

Some of the best forms of exercise to do during pregnancy are:

-   swimming
-   walking
-   gardening
-   [prenatal yoga](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/first-trimester-exercise-fitness#yoga4)
-   jogging

On the flip side, you should avoid any activities that:

-   rely on balance, such as bike riding or skiing
-   involve high temperatures
-   cause you pain
-   make you dizzy
-   involve lying your back (after 12 weeks of pregnancy)

### 3\. Address weight concerns early

Be sure to speak with your doctor and come up with a plan early on. Your doctor can refer you to a dietitian for more advice and meal planning, if necessary.

In addition, if you have overweight and not yet pregnant, [research](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8787851/) suggests that losing weight before you become pregnant can lower your chances of developing gestational diabetes.

## 

Frequently asked questions

Here are a couple of frequently asked questions about pregnancy and weight loss.

### Why am I losing weight during pregnancy?

There are a few reasons you may be unintentionally losing weight during pregnancy.

In the first trimester, it’s common to experience nausea and [morning sickness](/health/pregnancy/morning-sickness-peak/), which can make it difficult to eat. In addition, if you have an underlying medical condition that isn’t well managed, such as [type 1 diabetes](/health/type-1-diabetes-causes-symtoms-treatments), you may experience unintentional weight loss during pregnancy.

### Do you lose weight right before labor?

You can lose weight right before labor, but this is usually a small loss of 1 to 5 lbs.

## 

Takeaway

For most pregnant people, [weight maintenance](https://www.healthline.com/health/exercise-and-weight-loss) is safer than any form of significant weight loss.

It can be beneficial to monitor your calorie intake and to exercise during pregnancy. But overdoing it could potentially harm your baby.

This is why most doctors don’t recommend weight loss during pregnancy unless you have significant extra weight. Discuss any questions or concerns you have with your doctor.

Your doctor can help you make the safest decision for yourself and your baby. You can always revisit a weight loss plan after your baby is born.

# The Gluten-Free Diet: A Beginner’s Guide with Meal Plan

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Jared-Meacham-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Jared Meacham, PhD., RD, CSCS — Written by Ryan Raman, MS, RD and Rachael Ajmera, MS, RD — Updated on January 22, 2025

-   [Gluten-related disorders](#gluten-related-disorders)
-   [What to avoid](#what-to-avoid)
-   [What to eat and drink](#what-to-eat-and-drink)
-   [Sample meal plan](#sample-meal-plan)
-   [Health benefits](#health-benefits)
-   [Potential downsides](#potential-downsides)
-   [6 tips](#6-tips)
-   [Takeaway](#takeaway)

A gluten-free diet involves excluding foods that contain the protein gluten, which is found in wheat, barley, rye, and spelt. This diet can include foods like fruits, vegetables, meats, and gluten-free products.

Gluten is a protein that gives flour a [sticky consistency](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK538505/) when it’s mixed with water. It’s also associated with the rise and chewy texture of bread. The term “gluten” is derived from the Latin word for glue.

Gluten is commonly found in products such as bread, pasta, and baked goods.

A healthcare professional may recommend a gluten-free diet if you experience uncomfortable gastrointestinal symptoms after eating foods that contain gluten.

In some cases, you may need to completely avoid gluten if you have certain health conditions or are allergic to products that commonly contain gluten.

Keep reading to learn more about eating a gluten-free diet, including a sample menu.

## 

Who should eat a gluten-free diet?

Most people can eat gluten without experiencing any side effects. However, some people who have gluten-related disorders may need to avoid it. These disorders include:

-   [celiac disease](https://www.healthline.com/health/celiac-disease-sprue)
-   [non-celiac gluten sensitivity](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/gluten-sensitivity-is-real)
-   gluten [ataxia](https://www.healthline.com/health/ataxia), (an autoimmune disorder that may cause neurological symptoms, including issues with balance, motor control, and coordination)

If you have a [wheat allergy](https://www.healthline.com/health/allergies/wheat), you may not necessarily need to avoid gluten specifically, but you’ll need to avoid certain gluten-containing foods, including wheat-based products like bread, pasta, and baked goods.

Talk with a healthcare professional if you experience any of the following symptoms after eating foods that contain gluten:

-   [diarrhea](https://www.healthline.com/health/diarrhea)
-   [constipation](https://www.healthline.com/health/constipation)
-   [abdominal pain](https://www.healthline.com/health/abdominal-pain) or discomfort
-   [bloating](https://www.healthline.com/health/abdominal-bloating)
-   [unexplained weight loss](https://www.healthline.com/health/unexplained-weight-loss)
-   [skin rashes](https://www.healthline.com/health/dermatitis-herpetiformis)
-   [fatigue](https://www.healthline.com/health/fatigue)
-   [headaches](https://www.healthline.com/health/headache)
-   [difficulty breathing](https://www.healthline.com/health/what-does-shortness-of-breath-feel-like)
-   [nasal congestion](https://www.healthline.com/health/nasal-congestion)
-   swelling or irritation of your mouth or throat

A doctor can recommend [screening for gluten-related conditions](https://www.healthline.com/health/gluten-intolerance-test) and develop the best plan for you.

Learn more about [21 common signs of gluten intolerance](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/signs-you-are-gluten-intolerant).

## 

Foods to avoid when gluten-free

Completely avoiding gluten can be challenging, as it’s found in many common ingredients that are added to foods and beverages.

Wheat is the main source of gluten in the diet. Wheat-based products may [include](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053163/):

-   wheat bran
-   wheat flour
-   spelt
-   durum
-   kamut
-   semolina

Other common gluten sources [include](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK538505/):

-   barley
-   rye
-   malt

Below are some items that may have [gluten-containing ingredients](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/gluten-food-list) added to them:

-   **Bread:** all wheat-based breads
-   **Pasta:** all wheat-based pastas
-   **Cereals:** most types of cereal, unless they’re labeled gluten-free
-   **Baked goods:** cakes, cookies, muffins, bread crumbs, pastries
-   **Snack foods:** candy, granola bars, crackers, packaged convenience foods, roasted nuts, flavored chips, pretzels
-   **Sauces:** soy sauce, teriyaki sauce, hoisin sauce, marinades, salad dressings
-   **Beverages:** beer and some flavored alcoholic beverages
-   **Other items:** pizza, couscous, broth (unless it’s labeled gluten-free)

Many gluten-free products are now widely available, but it’s important to read the ingredient section of the food label on most of the foods you buy.

Labels may include additional statements such as “processed in a facility that also processes wheat-based foods.” [Oats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/gluten-in-oats) are naturally gluten-free but may contain traces of gluten if they are [processed](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0963996917308037?via%3Dihub) in the same factory as wheat-based foods.

## 

What to eat and drink

Gluten-free diets can be expensive and may lack important nutrients if not properly planned. There are plenty of [gluten-free options](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/gluten-free-foods) that will allow you to enjoy healthy and delicious meals.

The following items are naturally gluten-free:

-   **Meat, fish, and poultry:** all types, except battered or coated meats
-   **Eggs:** whole eggs, egg whites, egg yolks
-   **Dairy:** unflavored dairy products, including plain milk, yogurt, and cheese
-   **Fruits:** berries, melons, pineapples, bananas, oranges, pears, peaches, etc.
-   **Vegetables:** broccoli, tomatoes, onions, peppers, mushrooms, asparagus, carrots, potatoes, etc.
-   **Grains:** quinoa, rice, buckwheat, tapioca, sorghum, corn, millet, amaranth, arrowroot, teff, oats (if they’re labeled gluten-free)
-   **Starches and flours:** potato flour, cornstarch, corn flour, chickpea flour, soy flour, almond meal or flour, coconut flour, tapioca flour
-   **Nuts and seeds:** almonds, walnuts, pistachios, cashews, hemp seeds, chia seeds, flaxseed, etc.
-   **Spreads and oils:** vegetable oils, olive oil, coconut oil, butter, margarine, etc.
-   **Herbs and spices:** black pepper, turmeric, oregano, thyme, rosemary, parsley, cilantro, etc.
-   **Beverages:** most beverages, except for beer (unless it’s labeled gluten-free)

If you’re unsure whether an item contains gluten, read the nutrition label carefully. It could also be helpful to work with a registered dietitian to ensure that you’re meeting all your nutritional needs.

Learn more about [foods you can eat on a gluten-free diet](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods-you-can-eat-on-a-gluten-free-diet).

### Thursday

-   **Dinner:** grilled lamb with roasted vegetables

## 

Possible health benefits of a gluten-free diet

Most people with gluten-related disorders such as celiac disease try a gluten-free diet to [help treat](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3993) digestive problems such as:

-   bloating
-   diarrhea
-   constipation
-   gas
-   fatigue

Some research suggests that eating a gluten-free diet may also have other benefits for people with celiac disease and other gluten-related disorders, including:

-   [reducing](https://bmcmedicine.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12916-019-1380-z) markers of inflammation, such as antibody levels
-   reducing gut damage caused by gluten-related inflammation
-   [boosting](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jgh3.12134) energy levels and relieving tiredness that could be associated with nutrient deficiencies

It’s important to note that this diet is only necessary for those with celiac disease or other gluten-related disorders, and it may not be appropriate for everyone.

### Can you lose weight by going gluten-free?

You may experience some weight loss when you begin following a gluten-free diet.

This may be due to eliminating foods such as cookies, cakes, and other snack foods. However, some weight loss may occur as a result of the restrictive nature of the diet or a lack of planned balanced meals.

Gluten-free options are not always healthier or more nutritious than other foods. Certain gluten-free products, such as cakes, pastries, and snacks, may be high in calories and contain few beneficial nutrients.

This diet should not be considered a weight loss diet.

## 

Potential downsides of a gluten-free diet

A gluten-free diet may have several downsides for people who have a gluten-related disorder, as well as for those who have not received a diagnosis that requires the elimination of gluten.

### Risk of a nutritional deficiency

Some research suggests that gluten-free diets are [more likely](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7144711/) to be deficient in important nutrients such as:

-   [protein](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/protein-deficiency-symptoms)
-   [folate](https://www.healthline.com/health/folate-deficiency)
-   fiber
-   [zinc](https://www.healthline.com/health/zinc-deficiency)
-   [selenium](https://www.healthline.com/health/selenium-deficiency)
-   [iron](https://www.healthline.com/health/iron-deficiency-anemia)
-   [niacin](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/niacin-benefits)
-   [riboflavin](https://www.healthline.com/health/vitamin-watch-what-does-b2-do)
-   [thiamin](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/thiamine-deficiency-symptoms)
-   [vitamin B12](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-b12-deficiency-symptoms)

This may be because many gluten-free versions of foods are not fortified with these nutrients. Gluten-free diets are also [typically higher](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8009084/) in saturated fats, sodium, and carbohydrates than their gluten-containing counterparts.

A registered dietitian can help you plan balanced meals that meet your nutritional needs. They may recommend that you take vitamin and mineral supplements if you have trouble getting enough of these nutrients from food.

### Constipation

According to a [2021 study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32890754/), people who follow a gluten-free diet may also be more likely to experience constipation.

A fiber-rich diet may [help promote](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK559033/) healthy [bowel movements](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/fiber-and-constipation-truth). But gluten-free diets eliminate many popular sources of fiber, such as bread, bran, and other wheat-based products.

Some gluten-free substitutes for wheat-based products are also low in fiber.

### Cost

Following a gluten-free diet can be difficult if you’re on a tight budget.

A 2024 study found that gluten-free pastas and breads sold in U.S. grocery store chains were about [2.7 and 4.6 times more expensive](https://www.jacionline.org/article/S0091-6749\(23\)02148-6/fulltext), respectively, than their gluten-containing counterparts.

This is because gluten-free products cost manufacturers more money to make. For example, gluten-free foods must pass stricter testing and avoid cross contamination.

When following a gluten-free diet, you may be able to save money by:

-   eating produce items that are in season
-   choosing frozen or canned fruits and veggies
-   buying grain items in bulk
-   using beans and legumes as high fiber protein choices

### Difficulties when socializing

A [2021 review](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/13/11/3993) suggests that following a gluten-free diet could make it more difficult for people to socialize and engage in gatherings. This could lead to feelings of isolation, loneliness, and unhappiness.

Following a strict gluten-free diet may also make traveling and eating at restaurants more challenging. As a result, people who eat a gluten-free diet are more likely to stay home to eat.

It’s important to remember that you can still socialize while following a gluten-free diet — it simply requires a little extra preparation.

If you’re eating out, consider calling the restaurant beforehand to see whether gluten-free options are available. If you’re going to a social gathering, consider eating ahead of time or bringing your own food.

## 

Helpful diet tips

These tips can help you follow a gluten-free diet successfully:

-   **Read food labels:** Practice reading [food labels](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-read-food-labels) so you can easily identify gluten-free foods.
-   **Tell your friends:** If your friends know you’re following a gluten-free diet, they’re more likely to choose restaurants with gluten-free options when you eat out.
-   **Buy a gluten-free cookbook**:**** [Gluten-free cookbooks](https://www.healthline.com/health/best-gluten-free-books-of-the-year) may help you be more creative with your cooking and make meals more enjoyable.
-   **Plan ahead:** If you’re traveling, researching places to eat and shop can be helpful. Otherwise, plan your diet around plenty of whole, single-ingredient foods such as fruits, vegetables, and lean proteins.
-   **Use separate cooking utensils:** If you share a kitchen with friends or family members, use separate cooking and cleaning equipment to help prevent cross contamination and negative side effects.
-   **Bring your own food**:**** If you’re visiting family, consider taking foods such as gluten-free bread and pasta with you to help you feel more included in family meals.

Remember, if you don’t have a gluten-related disorder, you don’t need to follow a gluten-free diet. While this diet has health benefits for some people, it may not be suitable for everyone.

## 

The bottom line

A gluten-free diet involves completely avoiding the protein gluten, which is often found in pasta, baked goods, and other wheat-based foods.

While a gluten-free diet can be restrictive, there are plenty of nutritious and delicious options available.

You can consider eating more fruits, vegetables, lean protein, dairy foods, and healthy fats to promote overall health and prevent nutrient deficiencies.

If you’re unsure about how to plan balanced meals, consider speaking with a healthcare professional or a registered dietitian. They could help you develop a meal plan and recommend any supplements you may need to ensure that you get enough essential nutrients.

# Healthy Eating During Pregnancy

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Kathy-Warwick-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Kathy W. Warwick, RDN, CDCES — Written by Catherine Clark — Updated on July 9, 2020

-   [Balanced diet](#balanced-diet)
-   [Essential vitamins](#essential-vitamins)
-   [Essential minerals](#essential-minerals)
-   [Takeaway](#takeaway)

![Vegetable assortment on a table for healthy eating during pregnancy and pregnancy diets](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/vegetable_assortmen_on_wood_surface-1296x728-header.jpg?w=1155&h=1528)[Share on Pinterest](https://www.pinterest.com/pin/create/button/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.healthline.com%2Fhealth%2Fpregnancy%2Fdiet-nutrition&media=https%3A%2F%2Fpost.healthline.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2020%2F07%2Fvegetable_assortmen_on_wood_surface-1296x728-header.jpg&description=Healthy%20Diet%20During%20Pregnancy "Share on Pinterest")

If you’re concerned about what foods to eat to keep you and your baby healthy while you’re pregnant, that’s totally normal. Don’t worry — it’s easier than you think once you know which foods to prioritize.

Good nutrition during pregnancy can help ensure that your baby gets the best start possible. The meal plan is a balanced one that provides lots of:

-   protein
-   complex carbohydrates
-   healthy types of fat
-   vitamins and minerals
-   fiber and fluids

A healthy pregnancy eating pattern contains much of the same balance of vitamins, minerals, and nutrients as [healthy eating patterns in general](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthy-eating-for-beginners).

The difference is that you need higher amounts to compensate for all the work your body is doing and baby’s additional needs.

If you already have healthy eating habits, it shouldn’t be too hard to make slight adjustments to ensure a healthy pregnancy. And if you’re starting from scratch on healthy eating? Not to worry — there are lots of healthy *and* yummy options.

## 

Balance and variety

If you’re pregnant, you only need to consume about [300 calories more per day](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5104202/).

The old adage that you need to “eat for two” doesn’t mean that you double your intake: The key is moderation and working with your healthcare team to find the right calorie and nutrition goals for you.

### Complex carbs

Whenever possible, eat [complex carbohydrates](https://www.healthline.com/health/food-nutrition/good-vs-bad-carbs), such as:

-   whole grain breads and pastas
-   vegetables
-   beans
-   legumes

Limiting their tempting but lower fiber, nutritionally deficient cousins, the [simple carbs](https://www.healthline.com/health/food-nutrition/simple-carbohydrates-complex-carbohydrates):

-   white bread
-   cookies
-   pretzels
-   chips
-   excess added sugar

### Protein

Your protein needs increase considerably during pregnancy and peak during your third trimester.

To ensure you’re getting enough protein throughout your pregnancy, be sure to add a protein-rich food source to every meal and snack.

Examples of good, protein-rich foods include:

-   eggs
-   nuts and nut butters
-   seeds
-   fish
-   chicken or turkey
-   beans
-   Greek yogurt and cheese
-   tofu
-   lean beef or pork

Try preparing some easy, [protein-rich portable snacks](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthy-high-protein-snacks) for when you’re on the go. And talk to your doctor if you have questions regarding your specific protein needs.

### Vegetables and fruits

Vegetables contain loads of nutrients you and your growing baby need such as:

-   vitamins A and C
-   beta-carotene
-   fiber
-   vitamin E
-   riboflavin
-   folic acid
-   B vitamins
-   calcium
-   trace minerals

Here are some tips for [getting more veggies into your meals](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/17-ways-to-eat-more-veggies) without going full-on rabbit. Try making veggie-based sauces and adding vegetables to smoothies, soups, casseroles, lasagnas, and guacamole.

### Grains and legumes

[Whole grains](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/9-benefits-of-whole-grains) and [legumes](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthiest-beans-legumes), such as dried peas and beans, and other healthy carbs like fruit and starchy vegetables should make regular appearances on your plate.

They provide [B vitamins](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/b-vitamins) and trace minerals, such as zinc selenium and magnesium. Grains and legumes are full of nutrients, including iron and the various B vitamins: thiamin (vitamin B-1), riboflavin (vitamin B-2), folate, and niacin.

Your little one needs these for the development of just about every part of their body. For instance, folate intake [significantly reduces](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Folate-HealthProfessional/) the risk of having a baby with [spina bifida](https://www.healthline.com/health/spina-bifida).

These foods supply energy for your baby’s development and help build the placenta and other tissues in your body. It’s all about teamwork when it comes to fueling both you and baby.

### Fiber

Think of [fiber](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-is-fiber-good-for-you) as your body’s plumber, keeping constipation and hemorrhoids at bay. Try to eat 20 to 35 grams of fiber per day from whole grains, veggies, legumes, and fruit. Be sure to increase your intake of fluids along with fiber for best results.

Some fun options to toss into recipes include:

-   oats
-   nuts
-   [avocados](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/12-proven-benefits-of-avocado/)
-   [chia seeds](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/11-proven-health-benefits-of-chia-seeds)
-   berries

Remember to check the nutrition panel and choose products made with whole grains that contain the most fiber per serving.

### Fat

Remember those trendy low fat diets from the ’90s? Long gone are the days of avoiding fat. While you don’t want to consume excessive amounts of fats, it’s also dangerous to eliminate all fat from your meals. A healthy balance is recommended.

High fat foods to limit include fried foods and packaged products containing trans fats. Greasy meals tend to make any nausea or heartburn worse.

Essential fatty acids are important, including [omega-3 fatty acids](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/what-are-omega-3-fatty-acids). Even saturated fats, once considered a fat to avoid, are now known to be [important for fetal development](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27013336/).

Follow the same guidelines as the general public when it comes to choosing healthy fats. Include more plant-based fat sources like canola, olive, and soybean oil, and limit trans fats.

Some sources of healthy fats include:

-   walnuts
-   avocado
-   pumpkin and sunflower seeds
-   chia seeds
-   flaxseed
-   fatty fish
-   olive oil

These foods provide the right types of fats to fuel your baby’s brain development.

### Salt

Salt intake is important during pregnancy, and limiting it usually isn’t necessary, [even if you already have high blood pressure](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29046520/). In fact, pregnant people often need more salt in their food to compensate for the growing baby, and [restricting your intake could be harmful](https://jbiomedsci.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12929-016-0233-8).

However, you don’t need to limit salt while pregnant, it’s important to limit unhealthy, processed salty foods such as fast food and processed meats.

Speak to your healthcare provider if you have questions about your salt intake during pregnancy.

### Fluids

Fluids are an important part of any healthy eating plan. You should consume at least 80 ounces (2.4 liters) per day, and more is better to [avoid dehydration](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/dehydration). Pregnant people need the extra fluid to support the extra blood and amniotic fluid produced.

If you’re a coffee fan, you should limit caffeinated drinks while pregnant to [not exceed 200 milligrams (mg)](https://www.acog.org/Clinical-Guidance-and-Publications/Committee-Opinions/Committee-on-Obstetric-Practice/Moderate-Caffeine-Consumption-During-Pregnancy) of caffeine per day, according to the American College of Obstetrics and Gynecology (ACOG).

Water also reduces your chance of constipation and the subsequent hemorrhoids that can develop from straining while you go.

The increased flow of urine also reduces your risk of developing a [urinary tract infection](https://www.healthline.com/health/urinary-tract-infection-adults#utis-and-pregnancy), which can be dangerous for you and your baby.

## 

What vitamins do I need during pregnancy?

If you choose to take [supplements during your pregnancy](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/supplements-during-pregnancy), make sure you read the labels of every bottle and work with your healthcare providers. It’s important to stay within the daily allowance.

Keep in mind that a [complete prenatal vitamin](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/best-prenatal-vitamins) should have a balance of the nutrients that you need, and taking additional supplements may give you more than the recommended daily dosing in total.

Always discuss any supplements or over-the-counter medications you wish to take with your doctor for individual advice.

### Choline

Choline is a vital nutrient during pregnancy and plays an important role in baby’s brain development. Choline helps to prevent developmental abnormalities of the brain and spine.

Some research suggests that the current choline recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for pregnancy (450 mg per day) is inadequate and that a choline intake closer to [930 mg per day](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29217669/) is optimal while you’re pregnant.

Keep in mind that most prenatal vitamins don’t contain choline, so look for one that does or take a separate choline supplement. Check with your healthcare provider to make sure the amount is right for you.

Here are some foods high in choline:

-   beef liver provides [356 mg per 3-ounce serving](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Choline-HealthProfessional/).
-   eggs ⁠— just two egg yolks provide nearly 300 mg of choline
-   mushrooms
-   soybeans
-   kidney beans

### Folic acid

Folic acid is the synthetic form of folate found in supplements. It’s an important vitamin that stimulates red blood cell formation and the production of important chemical signals in the nervous system. It’s also important in the process of making DNA.

Perhaps more importantly, folic acid, like folate, has been identified as a critical vitamin to prevent neural tube defects in your baby, such as spina bifida.

[ACOG](http://www.acog.org/Patients/FAQs/Nutrition-During-Pregnancy) recommends taking 400 micrograms (mcg) a day before you conceive, and receiving at least 600 mcg a day from all sources, including meals, during pregnancy.

Good sources of folic acid include:

-   cooked green leafy vegetables
-   beef liver, cooked
-   great northern beans
-   fortified cereal
-   avocado
-   asparagus
-   citrus fruits and juices

### Pantothenic acid (B-5)

This vitamin (B-5) is involved in many of the body’s regulatory and metabolic activities. The RDA for the average person is 4 to 7 mg.

[Pantothenic acid](https://www.healthline.com/health/vitamin-watch-what-does-b5-do) is present in:

-   meats, including chicken and beef
-   potatoes
-   whole grains
-   broccoli
-   egg yolks

### Riboflavin (B-2)

[Riboflavin](https://www.healthline.com/health/vitamin-watch-what-does-b2-do) is important for baby’s development and growth. The RDA for pregnant people is 1.4 mg and 1.6 mg for those who are breastfeeding.

A prenatal vitamin may be your most consistent source, but B-2 can be found in milk and dairy products, with smaller amounts present in soybeans, grains, and pork.

### Thiamine (B-1)

[Thiamine](https://www.healthline.com/health/vitamin-watch-b1-thiamine) is important for metabolism and development of the brain, nervous system, and heart. When you’re pregnant, you need increased amounts of many vitamins, including B-1.

The RDA for pregnant people is about 1.4 mg.

### Vitamin A

[Vitamin A](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-a-benefits) is critical for proper cell growth and the development of the eyes, skin, and blood, as well as immunity and resistance to infection.

### Vitamin B-6 (pyridoxine)

[Vitamin B-6](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-b6-benefits) is important for your body’s metabolism and for the development of the fetal brain and nervous systems. The RDA for pregnant people is 1.9 mg.

### Vitamin B-12

[Vitamin B-12](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-b12-foods) is found mainly in meats and dairy products. So it can be a problem for vegans or strict vegetarians. If you have dietary restrictions, make sure that your vitamin supplement has adequate B-12.

Nutritional yeast, fortified with B-12, is a great staple for vegetarians. It has a salty and savory flavor and tastes similar to Parmesan cheese. Here’s why “the nooch” is so popular for its [health benefits](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/nutritional-yeast).

### Vitamin C (ascorbic acid)

The body does not stockpile [vitamin C](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-c-benefits), so you need regular sources to fulfill your daily requirement. The RDA for pregnant people is 85 mg.

You can reach your goal through daily intake with these foods:

-   citrus fruits (add fresh lemon or lime juice to your water!)
-   berries
-   bell peppers
-   broccoli
-   lots of other fruits and veggies

### Vitamin D

Humans produce [vitamin D](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/vitamin-d-101) in response to sunlight. Vitamin D itself is found naturally only in some fish liver oils.

Since exposure to sunlight is variable and this vitamin is so important for pregnant people and growing children, all milk is now fortified with vitamin D per quart as regulated by the U.S. government.

Vitamin D supplements are especially important if you don’t drink milk. Your doctor can check vitamin D levels to guide supplementation if you are taking a supplement.

And if you are deficient in vitamin D? You’re not alone: About [40 percent of people in the United States](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6075634/) are too.

## 

What minerals do I need during pregnancy?

### Calcium

Calcium, [vitamin D’s life partner](https://www.healthline.com/health/8-fast-facts-about-calcium#vitamin-d), is important for strong bones and teeth, of course. But it’s also critical for proper development and function of the heart and other muscles, as well as for the blood clotting system.

Your baby demands a huge supply of calcium during development. Pregnant people need 1,000 mg of calcium, preferably in [two doses of 500 mg](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Calcium-Consumer/#:~:text=Calcium%20absorption%20is%20best%20when,and%20constipation%20in%20some%20people.), per day.

Calcium-rich foods include:

-   milk and dairy products
-   calcium-fortified orange juice and bread
-   canned fish with bones
-   calcium-set tofu
-   cooked beans
-   cooked dark leafy greens

Prenatal supplements usually contain only 150 to 200 mg of calcium. So, prenatal vitamins alone won’t provide sufficient calcium for you.

### Iodine

[Iodine](https://www.healthline.com/health/iodine-uses) is critical for the development and functioning of the thyroid gland and regulation of metabolism. The RDA for pregnant people is 220 mcg per day.

You can get iodine from:

-   fluoridated drinking water
-   iodized (table) salt
-   eggs
-   milk
-   brewer’s yeast

### Iron

Make sure to eat [iron-rich foods](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthy-iron-rich-foods) daily. Since many people, especially women, don’t get enough iron in their meals, iron is an important part of prenatal supplements.

Iron is often poorly absorbed from plant foods, which is why it’s difficult to reach the proper requirement. Talk to your doctor if you are prone to [iron-deficiency anemia](https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/pregnancy-week-by-week/in-depth/anemia-during-pregnancy/art-20114455). They may recommend a supplement.

Iron-rich foods include:

-   spinach
-   lentils
-   fortified cereals
-   red meats such as beef and lamb
-   kidney, lima, and navy beans

To improve the absorption of plant (or non-heme) iron, [pair iron-rich food with a vitamin-C-rich source](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/increase-iron-absorption). For example, add fresh sliced bell peppers or strawberries to your spinach salad.

### Magnesium

[Magnesium](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/magnesium-supplements) is an important element for teeth and bones, regulation of blood-sugar levels, and the proper functioning of body proteins. It’s also important for tissue growth and repair, and may play a role in reducing preterm delivery.

The recommended upper limit for magnesium for pregnant people is around 300 mg. A good eating plan usually provides enough magnesium, so it’s not present in most prenatal vitamins.

The best food sources of magnesium are:

-   seeds such as sunflower and pumpkin
-   wheat germ
-   tofu
-   almonds
-   yogurt

### Chromium

[Chromium](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/chromium-picolinate) is important for your baby’s development. You should get about 30 mcg per day. Foods that contain significant amounts of chromium include:

-   whole wheat bread
-   peanut butter
-   asparagus
-   spinach
-   wheat germ

### Copper

[Copper](https://www.healthline.com/health/heavy-metal-good-for-you-copper) stimulates the growth of cells and tissues, hair growth, and general metabolism. It’s a critical component of baby’s major systems: The heart and circulatory system, the skeleton, and the nervous system.

One mg of copper is recommended daily.

### Zinc

It’s recommended pregnant people take in 11 mg of [zinc](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/zinc), while breastfeeding women need a bit more: 12 mg. You can buy prenatal vitamins that contain zinc.

Zinc sources include:

-   red meat
-   seeds
-   nuts
-   beans

### Potassium

[Potassium](https://www.healthline.com/health/potassium) is a mineral that affects cellular function, fluid balance, and blood pressure regulation, as well as proper nerve and muscle function.

[Research from 2017](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5639605/) revised potassium intake recommendations to 4,000 mg daily for adults and pregnant people (slightly higher for people who are breastfeeding).

Prenatal vitamins can provide potassium, but potassium is present at high levels in foods such as:

-   bananas
-   avocados
-   cantaloupes
-   oranges
-   watermelons
-   dark leafy greens
-   meats
-   milk
-   grains
-   legumes
-   squashes

### Phosphorus

[Phosphorus](https://www.healthline.com/health/phosphorus-in-diet) is an important part of the development of the muscular, circulatory, and skeletal systems. The RDA is [700 mg](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Phosphorus-Consumer/) for [pregnant and breastfeeding people](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/supplements-during-pregnancy).

Sources include:

-   milk
-   yogurt
-   beans
-   seafood
-   nuts

## 

The takeaway

Taking [prenatal multivitamins](https://www.healthline.com/health/ritual-vitamins) will ensure that you get the basic requirements. But vitamin-packed, fresh foods will help your baby get the best start in life and keep you going strong.

You should always talk to your healthcare provider or dietitian if you are concerned about your eating plan. They can help you determine if you are getting enough nutrients.

# 15 Foods and Drinks to Avoid During Pregnancy

![](//i0.wp.com/post.healthline.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/01/Stacy-Henigsman-500x500-Bio.png?w=105&h=105)

Medically reviewed by Stacy A. Henigsman, DO — Written by Adda Bjarnadottir, MS, RDN (Ice) — Updated on March 12, 2025

Foods to avoid during pregnancy typically include those that carry a risk of infection, such as undercooked or raw meat or fish, but you’ll want to limit your intake of caffeine and processed foods, too.

It’s essential to eat well during pregnancy to nourish both yourself and your unborn child. You may have to give up some of your favorite foods, such as [sushi](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/can-pregnant-women-eat-sushi), [coffee](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/caffeine-during-pregnancy), or [rare steak](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/infections-listeria).

However, there are plenty of [foods you can eat during pregnancy](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/13-foods-to-eat-when-pregnant/) to stay healthy.

Here are 15 foods and beverages to avoid or minimize while pregnant.

## 

1\. High mercury fish

Mercury is a highly toxic element and can occur in polluted waters. In higher amounts, it can affect your nervous system, immune system, and kidneys. It [may also cause](https://www.cdc.gov/breastfeeding-special-circumstances/hcp/exposures/mercury.html?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/breastfeeding/breastfeeding-special-circumstances/environmental-exposures/mercury.html) serious developmental problems in children, with adverse effects even in lower amounts.

Large marine fish can accumulate high amounts of mercury, so it’s best to avoid [high-mercury fish](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/mercury-content-of-fish/) during pregnancy and nursing.

High-mercury fish to avoid include:

-   shark
-   swordfish
-   king mackerel
-   tuna (especially bigeye tuna)
-   marlin
-   tilefish from the Gulf of Mexico
-   orange roughy

**»MORE:**[Learn more about mercury poisoning.](https://www.healthline.com/health/mercury-poisoning)

## 

2\. Undercooked or raw fish

[Raw fish](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/can-pregnant-women-eat-sushi/), especially shellfish, have a high risk of containing bacteria or [parasites](https://www.healthline.com/health/parasitic-infections/) such as [norovirus](https://www.healthline.com/health/norovirus/), *Vibrio*, *Salmonella*, and *Listeria*.

Raw fish can become infected during handling, storing, and processing, including smoking or drying.

These types of infections can cause [dehydration](https://www.healthline.com/health/dehydration/) and weakness in you, and if you are pregnant, some can pass through the placenta to your baby, even if you don’t have any symptoms. According to [the FDA](https://www.fda.gov/food/people-risk-foodborne-illness/what-foodborne-illness-food-safety-moms-be), foodborne illnesses can increase the risk of preterm delivery, [pregnancy loss](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/stillbirth-vs-miscarriage-how-theyre-similar-and-how-theyre-different/), stillbirth, and other serious health problems.

The CDC notes that the chance of having a [listeria](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/infections-listeria/) infection is [higher during pregnancy](https://www.cdc.gov/listeria/risk-factors/) than at other times.

**»MORE:**[Is raw fish safe to eat outside of pregnancy?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/eating-raw-fish)

## 

3\. Undercooked or raw meat

Eating undercooked or raw meat can also [increase your risk](https://www.foodsafety.gov/people-at-risk/pregnant-women) of infection from bacteria or parasites, including Toxoplasma, E. coli, Listeria, and Salmonella.

Bacteria may threaten your own health and the health and safety of your little one.

Most bacteria occur on the surface of whole pieces of meat, but other bacteria may linger inside the muscle fibers.

Some whole cuts of meat — such as tenderloins, sirloins, or ribeye from [beef](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-red-meat-bad-for-you-or-good), lamb, and veal — may be safe to consume when not cooked all the way through. However, this only applies when the piece of meat is whole or uncut, and [completely cooked](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/healthiest-way-to-cook-meat) on the outside. During pregnancy, it’s best to avoid any undercooked meat.

Cut meat, including meat patties, burgers, minced meat, [pork](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/pork-internal-temp/), and poultry, is **never** safe to eat raw or undercooked.

**»MORE:**[Is raw meat generally safe to eat?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/eating-raw-meat)

## 

4\. Processed and deli meat

Hot dogs, lunch meat, pepperoni, and deli meat can also [become contaminated](https://www.cdc.gov/listeria/outbreaks/delimeats-7-24/investigation.html) with various bacteria during processing or storage. Cured meats, which are not cooked, may also have bacteria or parasites.

In addition, [processed meats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-processed-meat-is-bad/) can contain [high levels of sodium](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods-high-in-sodium/) and unhealthy fats.

It’s best to avoid deli meats and ensure that [processed meats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/why-processed-meat-is-bad) you cook, such as sausages, are cooked through.

**»MORE:**[Why is processed meat bad for you?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/eating-raw-meat)

## 

5\. Raw eggs

Raw [eggs](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-proven-health-benefits-of-eggs) can [contain](https://www.foodsafety.gov/people-at-risk/pregnant-women) Salmonella bacteria.

Symptoms of [salmonella infection](https://www.healthline.com/health/salmonella-enterocolitis/) include:

-   fever
-   nausea
-   vomiting
-   stomach cramps
-   diarrhea.

It may also cause cramps in the uterus, potentially leading to [preterm birth](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/third-trimester-preterm-delivery/) or stillbirth.

Foods that commonly contain raw eggs include:

-   lightly [scrambled eggs](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/eating-healthy-eggs/)
-   tiramisu
-   raw batter
-   hollandaise sauce
-   homemade mayonnaise
-   some homemade salad dressings
-   homemade egg nog
-   homemade ice cream
-   some homemade cake icings
-   eggs Benedict

Most commercial products containing raw eggs are made with pasteurized eggs and are safe to consume. However, always [read the label](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-read-food-labels) to make sure.

Make sure to always cook eggs thoroughly or use pasteurized eggs.

**»MORE:**[Are raw eggs generally safe to eat?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/eating-raw-eggs)

## 

6\. Organ meat

[Organ meats](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/organ-meats) provide a range of essential nutrients, such as iron, vitamin B12, vitamin A, zinc, selenium, and copper — all of which are good for you and your baby.

However, consuming too much preformed vitamin A, especially in the first trimester of pregnancy, can lead to congenital malformations and pregnancy loss.

Although this is mostly [associated](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6470929/) with vitamin A supplements, it’s best to eat just a few ounces a week of meats like liver or kidney.

**»MORE:**[What's the nutritional value of organ meats?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/organ-meats)

## 

7\. Raw sprouts

Raw sprouts, including [alfalfa](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/alfalfa), clover, radish, and mung [bean sprouts](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/bean-sprouts-nutrition/), are popular in salads.

However, the humid environment in which the seeds need to start sprouting is ideal for the growth of Salmonella, and it’s almost impossible to wash off.

For this reason, it’s best to avoid raw sprouts altogether, although sprouts are safe to consume when cooked, according to the [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/food/buy-store-serve-safe-food/selecting-and-serving-produce-safely).

**»MORE:**[What's to know about raw sprouts?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/raw-sprouts)

> **Quick tips for foods to avoid when pregnant**
> 
> -   Avoid high-mercury fish including shark, swordfish, tuna, and marlin.
> -   Raw fish and shellfish can be contaminated with bacteria and parasites. Some of these can cause adverse health effects and harm both you and baby.
> -   Raw or undercooked meat may contain harmful bacteria. As a general rule, meat should be cooked all the way through.
> -   Raw eggs may be contaminated with *Salmonella*, and may put you and your baby at risk. Be sure to thoroughly cook eggs before eating.
> -   Organ meat is a great source of iron, vitamin B12, vitamin A, and copper. To prevent consuming too much vitamin A limit your intake of organ meat to a few ounces once a week.
> -   Limit caffeine intake to under 200 mg per day, which is about 2 to 3 cups of coffee. High caffeine intake during pregnancy may limit baby’s growth and cause low birth weight.
> -   Raw sprouts may be contaminated with bacteria. Only eat them thoroughly cooked.
> -   Fruits and vegetables may be contaminated with harmful bacteria, including *Toxoplasma*. It’s important to thoroughly wash all fruits and vegetables with plenty of clean water.
> -   Don’t consume unpasteurized milk, cheese, or fruit juice, as these foods increase the risk of bacterial infections.
> -   Avoid all alcohol. Drinking alcohol can increase the risk of miscarriage, stillbirth, and fetal alcohol syndrome.
> -   Eating processed foods during pregnancy can increase your risk of excess weight gain, gestational diabetes, and complications. This can have long-term health implications for you and your child.

## 

8\. Unwashed fruits and vegetables

The surface of unwashed or unpeeled [fruits](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/is-fruit-good-or-bad-for-your-health) and vegetables may be contaminated with bacteria and parasites such as Toxoplasma, E. coli, Salmonella, and Listeria. These can come from the soil or through handling.

Contamination can occur at any time during production, harvest, processing, storage, transportation, or retail.

Toxoplasma is a parasite that can linger on plant foods. Most people have no symptoms, but the parasite can [cross the placenta and cause](https://www.cdc.gov/toxoplasmosis/causes/) vision loss and learning difficulties later in life. In some cases, severe eye or brain damage may be present at birth.

To minimize the risk of infection, thoroughly wash all fruits and vegetables with clean water and peel or cook them before eating.

**»MORE:**[It is ever a good idea to drink raw milk?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/drinking-raw-milk)

## 

9\. Unpasteurized dairy products

[Raw milk](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/drinking-raw-milk) and other unpasteurized dairy products can [contain](https://www.fda.gov/food/buy-store-serve-safe-food/dangers-raw-milk-unpasteurized-milk-can-pose-serious-health-risk) harmful bacteria, including Listeria, Salmonella, E. coli, and Campylobacter. These bacteria can cause a range of infections commonly called food poisoning.

These infections can all have life threatening consequences for an unborn baby.

The bacteria can occur naturally or result from contamination during collection or storage. Pasteurization can kill any harmful bacteria without changing the nutritional value of the products.

To reduce the risk of infections, eat only pasteurized dairy products.

## 

10\. Soft cheeses

Some soft cheeses contain [listeria](https://www.cdc.gov/listeria/risk-factors/?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/listeria/risk-groups/pregnant-women.html), a type of bacteria that can cause severe illness and pregnancy loss.

Examples include:

-   feta
-   brie
-   queso fresco
-   queso blanco
-   queso blando
-   queso panela
-   queso ranchero

Only eat soft cheeses that have a label to show they are pasteurized.

**»MORE:**[What cheeses are safe to eat during pregnancy?](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/cheese-pregnancy)

## 

11\. Processed foods

Highly [processed foods](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/junk-food-vs-healthy-food/) tend to be low in nutrients and high in calories, [sugar](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/10-disturbing-reasons-why-sugar-is-bad), and added fats, and they may increase the risk of weight gain.

During pregnancy, you need a good intake of nutrients, such as [protein, folate, choline, and iron](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/nutrition).

Also, while some [weight gain](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/nutrition#weight-gain) is necessary, excess weight gain [can increase the risk](https://www.cdc.gov/maternal-infant-health/pregnancy-weight/) of delivery complications and childhood obesity.

Stick to meals and snacks that focus on protein, vegetables and fruits, [healthy fats](https://www.healthline.com/health/food-nutrition/healthy-fats-guidelines/), and fiber-rich [carbohydrates](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/12-healthy-high-carb-foods/) like whole grains, beans, and [starchy vegetables](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/starchy-vs-non-starchy-vegetables/).

**»MORE:**[Here are 17 ways to sneak veggies into your meals without sacrificing taste.](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/17-ways-to-eat-more-veggies)

## 

12\. Some fruit juices and smoothies

Fruit juices can be beneficial during pregnancy but opt for pasteurized juices with no added sugar.

Raw juices, like those squeezed on market stalls, [may contain](https://www.fda.gov/food/people-risk-foodborne-illness/fruits-veggies-and-juices-food-safety-moms-be) harmful bacteria. Smoothies may also contain unpasteurized juice, so ask about the ingredients beforehand if you’re in a restaurant.

**»MORE:**[Here are 7 fruits that are good to eat during pregnancy.](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/best-fruits-to-eat)

## 

13\. Alcohol

During pregnancy, alcohol [increases the risk](https://www.cdc.gov/alcohol-pregnancy/about/) of pregnancy loss, stillbirth, and [fetal alcohol syndrome (FAS)](https://www.healthline.com/health/fetal-alcohol-syndrome). FAS can affect many aspects of development, including the heart and the brain.

Since no level of alcohol has been proven to be safe during pregnancy, it’s best to avoid it altogether.

**»MORE:**[Learn more about pregnancy, alcohol, and drugs.](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/alcohol-drugs)

## 

14\. Caffeine

[Coffee](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/top-13-evidence-based-health-benefits-of-coffee), [tea](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/top-10-evidence-based-health-benefits-of-green-tea), soft drinks, and cocoa all contain [caffeine](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/foods-with-caffeine/).

Research has linked a high intake of caffeine to a risk of pregnancy loss, stillbirth, small weight at birth, and various developmental issues.

Caffeine is absorbed very quickly and passes easily into the placenta. Because babies and their placentas don’t have the main [enzyme](https://www.healthline.com/health/why-are-enzymes-important/) needed to metabolize caffeine, high levels can build up.

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends limiting caffeine intake to less than [200 milligrams (mg)](https://www.acog.org/Clinical-Guidance-and-Publications/Committee-Opinions/Committee-on-Obstetric-Practice/Moderate-Caffeine-Consumption-During-Pregnancy) per day during pregnancy.

**»MORE:**[How much caffeine is safe in pregnancy?](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/caffeine-during-pregnancy)

## 

15\. Contaminated drinking water

It’s essential to [drink plenty of water](https://www.healthline.com/health/pregnancy/how-much-water-should-a-pregnant-woman-drink) during pregnancy to avoid dehydration.

Most tap water in the United States is [safe to drink](https://www.cdc.gov/drinking-water/about/), but contaminants in unclean or polluted [drinking water](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/how-to-drink-more-water/) can harm you or your unborn baby. This may affect you if you use water from a private well, as the Environmental Protection Agency does not test this water.

Contact your local health authority or environmental agency if you have concerns about your water.

**»MORE:**[Is tap water safe to drink?](https://www.healthline.com/health/is-tap-water-safe-to-drink)

The bottom line

When you’re pregnant, it’s essential to avoid foods and beverages that may put you and your baby at risk.

Although most foods are safe to enjoy, it’s best to avoid items such as raw fish, unpasteurized dairy, alcohol, and high-mercury fish. You should also limit your intake of processed foods, as they can be [high in added sugar](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/18-surprising-foods-high-in-sugar/), salt, and unhealthy fats.
